The hammer is the most dramatic moment in golf betting. One word, mid-hole, and suddenly a $2 point is worth $16.

If you've watched TGL — Tiger Woods and Rory McIlroy's indoor simulator league — you've seen it in action. A team throws a hammer on a par 4, the other team has to decide in seconds: take it and play for double, or drop it and concede the hole. The tension is real, the math moves fast, and the crowd goes sideways.

Here's the thing though: the hammer isn't TGL's invention. Golf groups have been playing it for decades. Your group can throw hammers too — and if you play Wolf or Scotch, you may already have it as a built-in option without knowing the name.

This guide explains the mechanic clearly, shows you how TGL's version compares to recreational play, and walks through a real settlement example with the math.

---

## The Short Version

The hammer doubles the stakes on the current hole. Either team can throw it at any point while the hole is being played. The other team can take it (accepting the double) or drop it (conceding the hole at the current value). If they take it, they can immediately re-hammer, doubling again.

That's it. A $5 hole becomes $10, then $20, then $40. You can keep going as long as both teams keep taking.

## How TGL Uses the Hammer

TGL has a specific ruleset that's slightly different from recreational golf. [Golf Monthly has a detailed breakdown of TGL's hammer rules](https://www.golfmonthly.com/news/what-is-the-hammer-tgl) if you want every edge case. The short version: In TGL:

- Each team gets **three hammers** per match to use however they choose
- A hammer can be thrown **before a hole begins** (mandatory accept — the other team must take it no matter what) or **during a hole** (optional — the receiving team can decline)
- Throwing a hammer **before the first shot** locks the other team in
- Hammers increase the point value of a hole by one — so the maximum value of a single hole is three points if both teams use their hammers on it
- Once you've used all three, they're gone for the match

TGL teams treat hammer usage as a genuine strategic resource. How well you deploy your three hammers — and how well you respond when the other team throws one — often decides close matches. In TGL Season 2, hammer differential was one of the strongest predictors of match outcomes.

## How Your Group Plays It

In recreational golf, the hammer typically comes up in two games: Wolf and Scotch. The mechanics are similar but the framing differs slightly.

### Hammer in Wolf

Wolf is a four-player rotation game where one player is the Wolf each hole — they choose a partner after seeing drives, or go it alone. The hammer layers on top of the Wolf setup.

At any point while a hole is being played, either side can throw a hammer. The other side takes it (stakes double) or drops it (concedes the hole at current value). If they take it, they can re-hammer — teams alternate, so you can't throw two in a row.

Wolf already has multipliers built in: Lone Wolf doubles the stakes, Blind Wolf triples. Add a hammer into a Lone Wolf hole and the multipliers stack.

**Example:** The base point value is $2. Wolf goes Lone Wolf (2× multiplier = $4 per point). The other team throws a hammer mid-approach. Wolf's team takes it ($8). The opposing team re-hammers ($16). Wolf's team drops — they concede the hole at $16 per point. If all three opponents each collect one point, that's $48 on a single hole of a $2 Wolf game.

Most groups set a cap on the total multiplier when they have hammer enabled. Without a cap, a Lone Wolf hole with two re-hammers is 8x the base. That's fine if everyone's comfortable with the stakes, but it's worth agreeing to a ceiling before you start.

### Roll/Hammer in Scotch

Scotch is a four-player team game with six scoring categories per hole: low ball, low total, birdie, eagle, high ball, and proximity. The "roll" (some groups say "hammer") works the same way — either team can roll at any point, doubling the hole's point value. The other team takes it or drops it.

In Scotch, the roll is used less often than in Wolf — the game's point system means you're usually already tracking multiple values per hole, and a roll affects all of them. But it's a clean way to escalate when you're trailing and need a big hole.

## Hammer vs. Press: What's the Difference?

First-time players sometimes confuse the hammer with a press. They're different mechanics.

A **press** is what you do in Nassau or Match Play when you're down. You're 2-down on the back nine and you press — a new, parallel bet starts running alongside the original. The original bet keeps going. You're not doubling the current hole; you're starting a second simultaneous wager.

A **hammer** affects only the current hole. You throw it mid-hole, the other team takes or drops, and you move on. The hammer doesn't follow you to the next hole.

Put simply: a press extends across multiple holes. A hammer is over when the hole is over.

## A Full Settlement Example

Let's walk through a hole with hammer action so the math is concrete.

Justin, Jason, Evan, and Todd are playing Wolf at $2 per point. Justin is the Wolf on hole 7. He goes Lone Wolf — no partner, playing against all three.

**Standing before hole 7:** Justin is +2 points on the round.

**On the tee:** Everyone hits. Justin striped it down the middle. The other three are in the rough. Justin calls Lone Wolf. Multiplier: 2×.

**Mid-hole, after second shots:** Justin's approach came up short of the green. He's in a tough spot. Jason, who had the best of the three rough shots, is pin-high. Sensing a shift, Jason's team throws a hammer.

- Base hole value: $2
- After Lone Wolf multiplier: $4 per point
- Hammer on the table: $8 per point if Justin's team takes it

Justin's team has a choice: take the $8 and play on, or concede the hole at $4 per point.

Justin looks at his lie and takes it. He thinks he can get up and down. The $8 is live.

**The result:** Justin chips to six feet and misses. Jason makes par. Justin's team loses the hole.

**Settlement:** 1v3, Justin loses. He owes each opponent 1 point at $8 per point.
- Justin: −$24 (owes $8 to each of Jason, Evan, Todd)
- Jason: +$8
- Evan: +$8
- Todd: +$8
- Total: $0. Zero-sum confirmed.

That one decision — taking the hammer — turned a $12 loss (drop at $4/point × 3 opponents) into a $24 loss ($8/point × 3 opponents). Justin doubled his downside the moment he said "take it." That's the hammer.

## When to Throw It

Knowing when to reach for the hammer is half the game.

**Throw it when you have the edge.** If your team is sitting pretty — good lies, momentum, opponent scrambling — throw the hammer early. Make them decide when they're uncomfortable. A hammer thrown before they've hit their second shots forces a decision on incomplete information.

**Take it when you're confident, not when you're stubborn.** Taking a hammer out of pride is expensive. If you're in the rough and the other team is on the green, dropping the hole and conceding the current value is often the right call. Ego hammers are how $2 Wolf rounds become $80 Wolf rounds.

**Don't forget re-hammering.** If you take a hammer, you can immediately throw it back. If the original throw came from a position of confidence, re-hammering might shake them. Some teams throw, get taken, and haven't thought about the fact they're now on the wrong end of a re-hammer.

**Set ground rules first.** The biggest source of hammer arguments is forgetting to agree on limits. Before you start: is there a maximum multiplier? Can you hammer before tee shots? What happens on a tied hole — does the hammer carry? Settling these on the first tee takes two minutes and saves arguments on the back nine.

## FAQ

**What does "hammer" mean in golf?**
In golf betting, a hammer is a mid-hole double. Either team can throw it at any point during the hole. The other team takes it (stakes double) or drops it (concedes the hole at the current value). If they take it, they can re-hammer, and stakes double again.

**Is the TGL hammer the same as the recreational hammer?**
Similar mechanic, different ruleset. In TGL, each team gets three hammers for the entire match to use on specific holes, and a hammer thrown before the first shot of a hole must be accepted. In recreational Wolf or Scotch, hammers can be thrown at any time during any hole with no per-match limit, and the receiving team can always accept or drop.

**Which golf games use the hammer?**
The hammer mechanic is most common in Wolf and Scotch. Some groups play standalone hammer games where every hole is subject to hammering. In Match Play, the equivalent mechanic is usually called a press — though a press and hammer are different things (see above).

**What's the difference between a hammer and a roll in Scotch?**
The same mechanic, different name. Groups that play Scotch tend to say "roll" — "rolling" the hole means doubling it. Groups that play Wolf tend to say "hammer." If your group switches between the two games, you may use both words for the exact same move.

**Can you throw the hammer before anyone tees off?**
In recreational play, most groups allow it — though some set a rule that the hammer can only be thrown after tee shots, to prevent pre-hole gamesmanship. Whatever your group decides, settle it before you start and you're fine.

**What happens if both teams drop on the same hole?**
The hole is halved at the current value. In carryover games like Wolf, a halved hole carries the points forward (the base value, not the hammered value — unless your group has agreed otherwise).
