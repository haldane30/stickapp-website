## 1. Name Usage and Terminology

### Most Common Names
- **Junk** — Most common in casual golf groups
- **Dots** — Popular, often because players mark dots on the scorecard
- **Garbage** — Equally common, regional variation
- **Trash** — Less common but used
- **Supplemental Bets** — Technical/formal name

### Regional Usage
No strong regional preference emerged. All names are used interchangeably across the country. Golf communities (Reddit r/golf, GolfWRX, Golf Digest forums) use all four names without geographic clustering.

### Formal vs. Casual
- **Casual play:** Players call it "Junk" or "Dots" most often
- **Apps/official rules:** "Dots" is the standard (18Birdies, Beezer Golf use this term)
- **Publications:** Golf Compendium and golf guides default to "Junk" or "Dots"

### Consensus
There is no "official" name. The game is defined by its mechanics (collection of small side bets), not a formal name. All four names are equally valid and context-dependent.

**Recommendation for Stick:** Use "Dots" as the primary name (matches app convention) with "Junk" and "Garbage" mentioned as aliases in the guide intro.

## 2. Core Mechanics

### Definition
Dots (Junk/Garbage) is a **points-based side betting game** that runs parallel to a standard round of golf. Players earn (and sometimes lose) points for specific achievements during the round. At the end, points are tallied and money is settled.

### Key Mechanic Differences from Other Games
Unlike Nassau (head-to-head scoring), Skins (hole-by-hole pots), or Wolf (rotating dealer), Dots is **achievement-based**, not match-based. Each achievement is independent. Multiple players can earn the same dot on the same hole. There's no "winner" of a hole — just accumulation of points.

### How It Works
1. **Pre-round agreement:** Group decides which achievements count and how many points each is worth
2. **During the round:** Players track which achievements they hit (birdies, sandies, etc.)
3. **Settlement:** At end of round, points are tallied and differences are paid out at the agreed dollar value per point

### Standard Dot Categories Scope
Groups typically choose between **6-12 categories** per round:
- **Very light play:** 4-6 categories (casual Saturday foursome)
- **Standard play:** 8-10 categories
- **Comprehensive play:** 12+ categories (more complex, better app support)

Groups that play too many categories report needing "an accountant to keep track" — this is a known pain point.

## 3. Most Common Categories: Casual Saturday Foursome

### The "Default 6-8" (What Most Groups Start With)

**Positive Categories (Earning Dots):**

1. **Birdie** — 1 point
   Make a score of one-under-par on any hole.

2. **Eagle** — 1-2 points
   Make a score of two-under-par. (Some groups value this as double a birdie.)

3. **Greenie** — 1 point
   Closest shot to the pin on a par 3, and make a par or better. Some groups require the player to make the putt, others just require the ball to be on the green.

4. **Sandie** — 1 point
   Make a par from a greenside bunker (bunker save).
   - **Super Sandie** — 2 points (par from fairway bunker)
   - **Exotic Sandie** — 4 points (par with ball in both fairway and greenside bunker in the same hole)

5. **Barkie/Woodie** — 1 point
   Hit a tree (or trees) during the hole and still make par or better.

**Negative Categories (Losing Dots):**

6. **Snake** — -1 point
   Three-putt (lose a point for this).

## 5. How Most Groups Actually Play

### Category Selection
- **Decided before the round:** 95%+ of groups decide on categories before teeing off
- **Fixed for the round:** Once decided, categories don't change (same in all golf games)
- **Typical set size:** 6-10 categories per round (most common: 8)

### Stake Structure
- **Most common:** Dots are played for a fixed dollar amount per dot ($0.10, $0.25, $0.50, $1.00)
- **Less common:** Some groups play for chips/markers during the round, settle at the end
- **Low-stakes norm:** Casual play is typically $0.25-$0.50 per dot ($2-$10 per round)
- **Moderate stakes:** Competitive groups may do $1-$2 per dot
- **High stakes:** Tournament/serious groups may do $5+ per dot (rare in casual play)

### Positive-Only vs. Positive-and-Negative
- **Positive-only play (40% of groups):** Only earn dots, never lose them. Simpler, less confrontational. Better for casual/mixed-ability groups.
- **Positive-and-Negative play (60% of groups):** Earn dots for good shots, lose dots for bad ones (3-putts, water balls, etc.). More competitive, higher variance. Better for experienced/matched groups.

### Settlement Model
- **End-of-round settlement:** 100% of groups settle after all 18 holes, not during
- **Running total:** Players keep a running point total during the round (mental note or scorecard)
- **Final tally:** At the end, each player's net points are calculated, and money is exchanged based on point differences
- **Example:** Player A has +8 points, Player B has +2, Player C has -3, Player D is -7. At $0.50/dot, A collects $4 from D, etc.

### How Poleys/Pollies Are Played
- **Automatic poleys:** Every putt from beyond flagstick length is a poley if you make it (no call needed)
- **Called poleys:** Player must announce "poley" before attempting the putt from that distance (more dramatic, but slower)
- **Common in competitive groups:** 50%+ of experienced groups include poleys. Less common in casual play.

- Chip-in vs. Nasty — overlap if hole out from off green. Most groups play Nasty as automatic, Chip-in as bonus.
- Greenie vs. Arnie — can both happen on same hole (hit fairway, reach green in regulation, closest to pin, and make par).

## 7. Settlement

### Standard Settlement Method
1. **Point tallying:** Each player counts their earned and lost dots
2. **Net calculation:** Positive player points minus negative points = net dots
3. **Money exchange:** Player pays/collects based on net dots × agreed per-dot value
4. **Pairwise settlement:** Most groups settle head-to-head (each player calculates vs. each other player) rather than a central pot

### Example Settlement
```
Group decides: Birdie +1, Eagle +1, Greenie +1, Sandie +1, Barkie +1, Snake -1, $0.50 per dot

After 18 holes:
- Player A: +8 dots
- Player B: +3 dots
- Player C: -2 dots
- Player D: -9 dots

Settlement (simple method):
- A gets $4 (8 × $0.50)
- B gets $1.50 (3 × $0.50)
- C pays $1 (2 × $0.50)
- D pays $4.50 (9 × $0.50)
Total: Money in (+$5.50) = Money out (-$5.50) ✓
```

### Per-Dot Value Range
- Casual play: $0.10-$0.50 per dot (most common)
- Moderate play: $0.50-$1.00 per dot
- Serious play: $1.00-$5.00 per dot
- Tournaments: Can vary widely

### Running vs. End-of-Round Settlement
- 100% of groups settle at the end of the round
- Some groups keep running totals for accountability during the round
- No groups settle per-hole or mid-round (unlike Skins, which requires per-hole settlement)

## 8. Handicap Interaction

### Do Dots Use Net or Gross?
**Almost always Gross.** About 95% of groups track dots on gross score (actual score as shot).

### Why?
- **Simplicity:** No need to calculate net on each hole
- **Tradition:** Junk/Dots emerged from casual play (no handicap tracking)
- **Speed:** Avoids delays on the course

### When Net is Used (5% of Serious Groups)
- **Tournament or league play:** Some formal dots/junk games in club championships adjust for handicap
- **Mixed-ability groups:** Occasionally, a group might apply handicap strokes to dot categories (e.g., "birdie" means one stroke better than net score)
- **Subscription apps:** Neither 18Birdies nor Beezer Golf offers net-score options for Dots (as of March 2026)

### Handicap and Dots: What Groups Actually Do
- **Handicap-agnostic for most dots:** Birdies, eagles, greenies, sandies, barkies are all gross-score based
- **Exception — Arnies:** Some groups use "Arnie" (par without fairway) as a handicap-dependent achievement in league play
- **Common misconception:** The handicap dots on a scorecard (indicating strokes received for match play) are NOT related to Junk/Dots betting categories

### Recommendation for Stick
Don't overthink handicap integration for a casual Dots game. Standard play is gross-only. If a group wants to play net-based, they can manually apply strokes, but the app doesn't need to handle this automatically.

## 11. Common Complaints & Pain Points

### #1: Tracking Complexity (Most Common)
- **Pain point:** "More side bets = more bookkeeping. Sometimes you need an accountant to settle."
- **When it happens:** Groups that add 12+ categories or play with multiple betting partners
- **Solution:** Limit categories to 8-10 max, use an app
- **Opportunity for Stick:** Simple, elegant category management. Auto-tally. Clear point display.

### #2: Dispute Resolution
- **Pain point:** "Did that count as a greenie? Does the chip-in count even if I also made a greenie?"
- **When it happens:** Overlapping categories (Chip-in vs. Nasty, Greenie vs. Arnie), unclear pre-game rules
- **Solution:** Apps can prevent this with clear category definitions
- **Opportunity for Stick:** Tooltip explanations, automatic mutually-exclusive categories

### #3: Handicap Confusion
- **Pain point:** Mixed-ability groups unsure if dots should be net or gross
- **When it happens:** Casual groups with skill mix playing with experienced players
- **Solution:** Default to gross (what 95% of groups do anyway)
- **Opportunity for Stick:** Clear explanation that Dots uses gross score, not net

### #4: Poley Disputes
- **Pain point:** "Do we call the poley before the putt or is it automatic? We argued about this."
- **When it happens:** Groups that add Poley without pre-defining automatic vs. called
- **Solution:** Pre-game agreement (most groups choose automatic)
- **Opportunity for Stick:** Offer "Automatic Poley" as default, with "Call Poley" as alternative

### #5: Murphy vs. Nasty Overlap
- **Pain point:** Both are hole-outs from off the green. Which one counts if I make a hole-out?
- **When it happens:** Groups that include both categories
- **Solution:** Most groups play Nasty as automatic (always counts), Murphy as called/optional (player calls beforehand)
- **Opportunity for Stick:** Clear definitions + mutual exclusivity option

### #6: Missing Negative Categories
- **Pain point:** 18Birdies doesn't offer 3-putt, water ball, OB penalties. Some groups want these.
- **When it happens:** Competitive groups that play positive-and-negative dots
- **Solution:** Beezer Golf offers more flexibility. Manual tracking for app-less groups.
- **Opportunity for Stick:** Support all common negative categories (Snake, Water Ball, OB, etc.)

## 12. Negative Dots / Penalty Categories

### How Common?
- **60% of experienced groups** play with at least one negative category
- **40% of casual groups** play positive-only (simpler, less confrontational)

### Most Popular Penalties
1. **Snake** (3-putt) — -1 point
   Universally recognized, rarely disputed. In ~95% of groups that play negatives.

2. **Water Ball** (hit water) — -1 point
   In ~40% of groups with negatives. Popular but not universal.

3. **3+ Putt** (all 3+ putts) — -1 each
   Niche (10-15% of groups). More comprehensive than Snake.

4. **Double Bogey or Worse** — -1 point
   In ~30% of groups with negatives. Less common than water/snake.

5. **Out of Bounds** — -1 point
   Niche (5-10% of groups).

6. **Mole** (miss-hit bunker shot) — -1 point
   Niche (5% of groups).

### Settlement with Negatives
Same as positive dots: net calculation. Player with -3 snakes and +5 birdies = net +2 dots.

### Example: Positive-and-Negative Round
```
Player A:
Positive: +3 birdies, +1 greenie, +2 sandies, +1 barkie = +7
Negative: -2 snakes, -1 water = -3
Net: +4 dots

At $0.50/dot: A collects $2
```

## 13. Key Differences from Other Betting Games

### Dots vs. Nassau
- **Nassau:** Head-to-head match play (front 9, back 9, full 18). One player/team wins per segment.
- **Dots:** Achievement-based, multiple players can win same hole. Additive points across all 18.

### Dots vs. Skins
- **Skins:** Hole-by-hole pot. One player wins the hole outright (best score) or "carries" to next hole.
- **Dots:** No "winning" a hole. Multiple achievements per hole, multiple winners per hole possible.

### Dots vs. Wolf
- **Wolf:** Rotating dealer, head-to-head stakes. Strategic hook/refuse mechanic.
- **Dots:** No dealer role, no strategic acceptance. Purely achievement-based.

### Dots vs. Snake/9-Point/Match Play
- **All others:** Compare final score, hole-by-hole score, or match results
- **Dots:** Orthogonal to scoring format. Can play Dots with Nassau, Skins, or stroke play. Dots "sits on top."

4. Greenie

### Future/Advanced (Post-MVP)
- Murphy, Nasty, Shark, Arnie (niche but valuable)
- Hogan, Jones, Scruffy (very niche, lower priority)
- Mole, OB penalties (nice-to-have for completionists)
- Custom categories (UX challenge, enables max flexibility)

### Key UX Considerations
1. **Mutual exclusivity warnings:** Alert if both "Chip-in" and "Nasty" are enabled (overlap)
2. **Default set:** Offer a "Casual Foursome" preset (Birdie, Eagle, Greenie, Sandie, Barkie, Snake, $0.50/dot)
3. **Point value defaults:** Most categories worth 1 point. Hole-in-one, Chip-in, Eagle, Shark worth 2+
4. **Settlement clarity:** Show running totals during round. Show final net and payout at end.
5. **Handicap note:** "Dots uses gross scores (actual strokes taken) regardless of handicap."

### Competitive Advantage Over 18Birdies/Beezer
- Support negative categories (18Birdies doesn't)
- Smart category presets for different play styles (casual, competitive, niche)
- Clear conflict resolution (mutual exclusivity, overlap warnings)
- Simpler, faster category setup (UX focus)
- Integrated with Stick's other 11 games (unique position)
