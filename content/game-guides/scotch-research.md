# Scotch Golf Betting Game Research

**Research Date:** March 2026
**Researcher Note:** This document covers Six-Point Scotch specifically (the most common form), distinguishes it from Scotch Foursomes and Split Sixes, and captures real-world variations golfers actually play.

---

## Executive Summary

**Scotch** (also called "Six-Point Scotch," "6-Point Scotch," or "6-Point Game") is a 4-player, 2v2 team betting game where players earn points on each hole based on four categories: low ball (lowest individual score), low total (lowest combined team score), closest to pin, and birdie. The standard distribution is 2-2-1-1 points respectively, totaling 6 points per hole. If one team wins all 6 points, those points double to 12 (called a "blitz" or "umbrella").

**Critical Distinction:** "Scotch" in golf betting contexts almost always refers to this points-based team game. "Scotch Foursomes" is an entirely different format (alternate shot with selected drive). "6-Point Game" can ambiguously refer to either Scotch (4-player) or Split Sixes (3-player individual), and this confusion is extremely common among golfers.

## 1. Names and Terminology

### Primary Names
Golfers refer to this game by several interchangeable names:
- **Scotch** (most common shorthand)
- **Six-Point Scotch** (explicit, clearest)
- **6-Point Scotch** (numeric variant)
- **6-Point Game** (ambiguous — see Section 1.2)
- **5-Point Scotch** (less common variant — see Section 4)

### Regional and Group Variations
- No strong geographic regional naming variation documented. Instead, variations are **club-specific and group-specific**.
- Each golf club and group of players tends to tweak the umbrella/blitz rule or other mechanics, suggesting Scotch is locally customized rather than regionally standardized.
- Terminology is consistent across North America (sources from Chicago, Toronto, Florida, and national outlets all use same language).

### The "6-Point Game" Ambiguity

**This is a major source of confusion.** "6-Point Game" can refer to two completely different games:

1. **Scotch (6-Point Scotch)** — 4-player team game, 2v2, points awarded across low ball/low total/prox/birdie
2. **Split Sixes** — 3-player individual game, 6 points per hole distributed 4-2-0 (low score gets 4, middle 2, high 0)

When someone says "let's play 6-point," golfers must clarify:
- "Is this Scotch?" (asking if 4-player team format)
- "How many players?" (4 = likely Scotch, 3 = likely Split Sixes)
- "How are points divided?" (Asking to confirm 2-2-1-1 distribution vs 4-2-0)

**Source:** Multiple guides confirm the confusion explicitly. Golf.com lifestyle piece titled "This golf gambling game is for three players (and risk-takers) only" addresses Split Sixes and notes the naming ambiguity.

## 2. The Critical Scotch vs. Scotch Foursomes Distinction

### Scotch (Points Game) — What Most Golfers Mean

**Format:** 4-player, 2v2 teams, best-ball scoring
- Both players on each team play their own ball for the entire round
- Each player's score contributes to the team
- Points are awarded on each hole across multiple categories
- This is the betting game played every weekend on public and private courses

**Scoring:** 2 points for low ball, 2 for low total, 1 for prox, 1 for birdie (6 total per hole)

### Scotch Foursomes (Alternate Shot) — A Completely Different Format

**Format:** 4-player, 2v2 teams, alternate shot (one ball per team)
- Players alternate hitting the same ball (Player A hits drive, Player B hits second shot, etc.)
- Only one ball per team is in play
- This is a stroke play format, not a points game

**Key Variants of Scotch Foursomes:**
- **Standard:** One player tees off on odd holes, partner on even holes
- **Modified (also called "Greensomes," "Canadian Foursomes," or "Pinehurst"):** Both players tee off, team selects best drive, then alternate shots from there

### Why the Confusion?

"Scotch" in the name of both games creates significant confusion. The name likely refers to Scotland/golf's Scottish roots. However:
- The **points-based Scotch game** is far more popular for casual weekend betting
- **Scotch Foursomes** is a tournament format used less frequently
- A golfer asking to "play Scotch" almost always means the points game, not alternate shot
- New players often confuse the two formats entirely

**Real-World Impact:** Multiple sources describe this as "golf's most popular and confusing gambling game." The Chicago Golf Guy explicitly titled one article "The Scotch Game: Golf's Most Popular and Confusing Gambling Game" specifically addressing the ambiguity.

## 3. The Points System

### Standard 6-Point Distribution (Most Common)

Per hole, 6 points are distributed as follows:

| Category | Points | Description | Notes |
|----------|--------|-------------|-------|
| **Low Ball** | 2 | Team with lowest individual score on hole | Whoever shoots the best single score (from either player on that team) |
| **Low Total** | 2 | Team with lowest combined score on hole | Both players' scores added together |
| **Closest to Pin** | 1 | Team with ball closest to pin in regulation | Only counts if ball reaches green in regulation (par-2: 1 shot, par-3: 2 shots, par-4: 3 shots, par-5: 3 shots) |
| **Birdie** | 1 | Team with a birdie (natural, not scramble) | Only a natural birdie counts — 4-for-3 doesn't count as birdie if it started as par-4. At least one team member must make an actual birdie. |

**Total: 6 points per hole available**

### Typical Hole Scenarios

**Scenario 1: One team dominates (6-0 sweep/blitz)**
- Team A shoots 4-5 (9 total). Team B shoots 6-7 (13 total).
- Team A gets: Low ball (2) + Low total (2) = 4 points minimum
- If Team A also made a birdie: +1 (birdie) = 5 points
- If Team A ball is closest to pin: +1 (prox) = 6 points
- **Result: Team A 6, Team B 0. The 6 points double to 12 (blitz/umbrella)**

**Scenario 2: Split (3-3 tie)**
- Team A shoots 4-5 (9 total). Team B shoots 4-6 (10 total).
- Low ball: Team A and Team B both have 4 → **Each team gets 1 point** (split the 2 points)
- Low total: Team A 9 beats Team B 10 → **Team A gets 2 points**
- Neither team made birdie or had closest to pin
- **Result: Team A 3, Team B 1** (points don't split on non-tie categories)

**Scenario 3: Points cancel (1-1, no blood)**
- Team A shoots 4-6 (10 total). Team B shoots 5-5 (10 total).
- Low ball: Team B's 5 beats Team A's 4 → **Team B gets 2 points**
- Low total: Tied at 10 → **Each team gets 1 point** (split the 2)
- No birdie, no prox
- **Result: Team A 1, Team B 3, but points cancel, so effectively Team B +1 for the hole** (or "no blood" if teams track cumulative)

### What Happens on Ties?

**If low balls are tied (both teams have same low score):**
- The 2 points for low ball are typically split evenly (each team gets 1)
- Some groups may have a house rule to split (1-1), others may have tiebreaker rules (e.g., "closest second shot wins")

**If low totals are tied:**
- The 2 points for low total are split (each team gets 1)

**If all four players tie with same score (rare):**
- Low ball points split (1-1), low total points split (1-1) = 2-2 on the hole
- Prox and birdie are still in play

**Important:** The research does not find comprehensive documentation on edge cases (e.g., what if one team's low ball is 3, but both teams tied at 3?). This suggests groups handle these via house rules.

## 4. Variations

### 7-Point Scotch (Birdie Bonus Variant)

Some groups add a seventh point for an additional birdie bonus or "scramble" point:

**7-Point Breakdown:**
- 2 points for low ball
- 2 points for low total
- 1 point for closest to pin
- 1 point for birdie (natural birdie)
- **+1 point for "scramble"** (less documented; some sources mention this as an optional 7th point)

If a team wins all 7 points, the points double to 14.

**Frequency:** Less common than 6-point; mentioned by Beezer Golf but not emphasized in most guides. Appears to be a **niche variation** some groups use to increase scoring opportunities.

### 5-Point Scotch

Less commonly documented but mentioned in some sources. Likely removes one point category (possibly closest to pin or birdie).

**Note:** The research finds 6-point most common, then 7-point as secondary, with 5-point rarely mentioned. The 6-point variant is the de facto standard.

### 4-Point Variant (Not Documented)

No sources found describing a 4-point version. All references center on 5-point (rare), 6-point (standard), or 7-point (occasional).

## 5. Handicap Application

### Method: 40% of Combined Handicaps

For a team game like Scotch, handicap allowance follows this formula:

**Team Handicap = 40% of (Partner A's course handicap + Partner B's course handicap)**

**Example:**
- Partner A: 12 handicap
- Partner B: 18 handicap
- Combined: 30
- Team allowance: 30 × 0.40 = 12 strokes

### How to Apply Strokes

1. Look at the scorecard's "HCP" (handicap) column
2. Find the highest-rated handicap holes (holes marked 1, 2, 3, etc. in the HCP column)
3. Apply one stroke to each of those holes in order
4. If team handicap is 12, apply strokes to the 12 holes rated as hardest (HCP 1-12)

### Standard: Gross vs. Net

**Scotch is typically played GROSS** (no handicap strokes applied to scoring). Golfers' actual scores determine low ball and low total.

**However:** Some clubs may play net (handicap-adjusted). This is a **house rule variation**. Most common format is gross scoring.

## 6. Settlement

### Betting Structure

Golfers set a dollar value per point **before the round starts:**

**Typical betting rates (friendly to aggressive):**
- $0.50/point (very casual)
- $1/point (standard friendly)
- $2/point (moderate)
- $5/point (aggressive)

### Cumulative Settling Method (Most Common)

1. **Track points per hole** throughout the round (cumulative for each team)
2. **At 18 holes, tally total points** for each team
3. **Calculate difference:** (Team A points - Team B points) = point differential
4. **Multiply by point value:** Point differential × $ per point = amount owed

**Example Settlement:**
- Betting $1/point
- Team A ends with 52 points, Team B ends with 46 points
- Differential: 52 - 46 = 6 points
- Team B pays Team A: 6 × $1 = $6

**Points cancel each other out.** If Team A wins 4 points and Team B wins 2 points on a hole, the net is Team A +2 for that hole (or teams track cumulatively).

### Per-Hole Settling Method (Less Common)

Some groups settle **hole by hole** with a fixed bet per hole:
- Set match bet (e.g., $1/hole, $2/hole)
- Whoever wins the points on that hole wins the hole bet
- Settle at every 9 holes or at end of round

This is more cumbersome and less common than cumulative.

### Typical Maximum Exposure

**No formal "maximum" rule** documented. However, typical exposure dynamics:

- **Per hole maximum before presses:** 6 points × $1/point = $6 per team
- **With umbrella (blitz):** 12 points × $1/point = $12 per team per hole (doubles the exposure)
- **With press:** Doubles the multiplier on that hole (6 points → 12 with press, or 12 → 24 with umbrella + press)
- **Typical 18-hole range:** $20-$50 per player depending on betting amount and presses

**Limiting exposure:** Groups often restrict presses (e.g., "one tee press per side") to prevent runaway betting scenarios.

## 8. Scoring: Net vs. Gross

### Standard Practice: Gross Scoring

Scotch is **almost always played gross** (actual scores, no handicap adjustment). This makes calculating low ball and low total straightforward.

**Reason:** Since the game distributes points for actual scores, adding handicap strokes would complicate daily calculations and fairness disputes.

### Potential Net Variant

Some upscale clubs or tournaments may apply handicaps (net scoring), but this is **not standard** in casual weekend games and is rarely discussed in golf betting guides.

## 9. Strategy and Tips

### Low Ball vs. Low Total Dynamic

The 2-2 split (2 points for low ball, 2 for low total) creates unique strategy:

- **Low ball incentivizes individual excellence** — one great shot helps your team
- **Low total incentivizes avoiding big numbers** — consistency is rewarded
- A team can win low ball (3-5 = 3 is low) but lose low total (8 vs. 7) → split 2-2 instead of sweeping

**Strategic implication:** Partners must balance "go for broke on this hole" (aggressive) vs. "play it safe and make par" (defensive).

### Pressing (When Used)

**Key rule:** Only the trailing team can press

- **Hole press:** Doubles the bet for that single hole (6 points become 12 if you sweep)
- **Game press:** Doubles the bet for the remaining holes (e.g., starting on hole 4, all holes 4-9 are now double)

**Important finding:** Pressing in Scotch is **often discouraged** in casual play. Reasons:
- The game's point escalation (winning multiple points per hole) already creates comeback opportunities
- Tracking multiple presses gets complex ("press on hole 3, another on hole 5, one game press on hole 7")
- Most sources say presses are "not typically used in Scotch games"

### General Strategy Tips

1. **Play to your strengths as a team** — One partner short game, other partner long game? Play accordingly
2. **Communicate constantly** — Discuss every shot with your partner (club selection, risk assessment)
3. **Know the matchups** — Understand which opponent is stronger off the tee, in short game, with pressure shots
4. **Avoid risky plays when you're already winning** — Lock in points on holes you're winning
5. **Get aggressive when you're down** — Trailing teams need to take calculated risks to catch up

### Partnership Pairing

Teams are typically paired by skill level:
- **Players 1 & 4** vs. **Players 2 & 3** (creates more even teams)
- Uneven pairings (1 & 2 vs. 3 & 4) create lopsided matches

## 10. Common Mistakes New Players Make

### 1. Confusing Scotch with Scotch Foursomes

New golfers often think Scotch is alternate shot. They don't understand that both players hit their own ball and that points are awarded across multiple categories. This is the **most common misunderstanding**.

### 2. Misunderstanding Birdie Point

**Error:** "If I make par, do I get the birdie point?"
**Correct:** Only a natural birdie (one under par) counts. A par doesn't earn the birdie point.

**Edge case:** If the hole is par-4 and you score 3, that's a birdie. If you score 4, that's par (no birdie point).

### 3. Overthinking Tie Settlement

New players often don't know how to split points when scores tie. Some assume ties mean "no one gets those points." Correct understanding:
- Tied low ball (both teams have same low score) → split the 2 points (each gets 1)
- Tied low total → split the 2 points

### 4. Forgetting Closest to Pin Only Counts in Regulation

New players sometimes award the prox point to a player who reached the green in 4 shots on a par-3 (out of regulation). Rule:
- Par-3: 1 shot to reach green
- Par-4: 2 shots (3 for par-5)
- Only one player per team counts toward low ball, and only that low ball player's proximity is in the running

Actually, **the prox point goes to whichever team has the ball closest to the pin, regardless of which player hit it** — but only if at least one player on that team reached in regulation.

### 5. Not Clarifying House Rules Before Starting

Since every group plays Scotch slightly differently (umbrella multiplier, press rules, handicap treatment), **failure to clarify house rules is a major source of disputes and confusion.**

### 6. Assuming Press Rules Without Asking

Some groups use presses heavily, others avoid them. Some restrict presses, others allow unlimited. Players must **ask about press rules explicitly before play.**

### 7. Confusing "6-Point Game" with Split Sixes

A golfer says "let's play 6-point." Without clarification:
- Is it Scotch (4-player)? → Yes, ask about pairing and house rules
- Is it Split Sixes (3-player)? → Completely different game structure

**Resolution:** Always ask "How many players?" and "What's the scoring breakdown?" when someone mentions "6-point."

## 12. FAQ Preparation (For Game Guide)

Based on research, these questions will be critical for the game guide's FAQ section:

1. **What's the difference between Scotch and Scotch Foursomes?**
   - Clear: Scotch = points game with best-ball scoring, Scotch Foursomes = alternate shot

2. **What does "umbrella" or "blitz" mean?**
   - Winning all 6 points on a hole doubles to 12

3. **What if two players tie with the same low score?**
   - The 2 points for low ball are split (each team gets 1)

4. **Does a "4-for-3" count as a birdie?**
   - No, only a natural birdie (one under par) counts

5. **Can you press in Scotch?**
   - Yes, but presses are less common in Scotch than other games because point escalation already creates comeback opportunities

6. **What's the typical betting amount?**
   - $0.50-$5 per point depending on stakes (friendly = $0.50-$1, aggressive = $2-$5)

7. **Is Scotch played net or gross?**
   - Gross (actual scores). Handicaps are rarely applied.

8. **What does "closest to pin" mean?**
   - The team whose ball lands closest to the pin in regulation (reaching the green in par or better)

9. **How is Scotch different from Skins or Nassau?**
   - Scotch awards multiple points per hole across categories; Skins is one point per hole winner-take-all; Nassau divides round into three separate matches

10. **What is "6-point game" and does it always mean Scotch?**
    - No, "6-point game" can mean Scotch (4-player) or Split Sixes (3-player). Always clarify player count and scoring breakdown.

11. **What if one team wins low ball but loses low total on the same hole?**
    - Both teams get points — Team A gets 2 (low ball), Team B gets 2 (low total), resulting in 2-2 split with neither sweeping

12. **Is there a maximum to how much you can win on a single hole?**
    - Standard maximum is 6 points per hole (12 if you get all 6 + blitz/umbrella). Presses can double this further, but most groups limit presses to control exposure.

## 13. Gaps and Contradictions Found in Research

### Gap 1: Tie-Breaking Rules for Prox

The research does not document what happens if two players (from different teams) are tied for closest to pin. Some sources may have house rules (e.g., "closest second shot wins"), but this isn't explicitly standardized.

### Gap 2: Birdie Bonus Point Definition

The "7-point" variant mentions a potential "scramble" point, but documentation is sparse. Unclear if this is:
- An extra point for eagle
- An extra point for birdie by both partners
- Something else entirely

### Gap 3: Prox Eligibility in Regulation

Most sources state prox "only counts if in regulation," but edge cases aren't covered:
- What if one team reached green in regulation, the other in 5 shots on a par-4?
- Does prox only count if **both teams** reached in regulation?

**Likely answer:** The team with closest ball wins prox as long as at least one team reached in regulation, but research doesn't explicitly confirm this.

### Gap 4: Handicap Treatment in Team Scenarios

While 40% of combined handicaps is documented for Scotch Foursomes, less clarity on whether this applies to Scotch points game or if gross scoring is always used. Most sources imply gross is standard, but one source hints at net variant possibility.

## 14. Key Findings for Stick Product Audit

When developing Scotch scoring for Stick, these real-world complexities will need to be handled:

1. **Tie handling:** Need to support point splits (1-1) when low ball or low total are tied
2. **Umbrella/blitz multiplier:** 6 points double to 12 when one team sweeps all categories
3. **Press mechanics:** While not heavily used, option for hole press and game press adds complexity
4. **Prox eligibility:** Only award prox point if at least one team reached green in regulation
5. **Birdie validation:** Only natural birdies count, not pars or scramble scores
6. **Settlement calculation:** Clean cumulative point tracking with final differential calculation
7. **Variant support:** At minimum 6-point (standard), with option for 5-point and 7-point
8. **House rule customization:** Since every group plays Scotch differently, need flexibility for:
   - Point distributions (if groups want 3-3 instead of 2-2-1-1)
   - Umbrella cap (some groups may want umbrella to 12, others to 24)
   - Press limits (restrict to X presses per side)
