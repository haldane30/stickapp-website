# Sixes Golf Game Research

**Last Updated:** March 1, 2026

---

## Executive Summary

"Sixes" is actually TWO distinct games that often confuse golfers:

1. **4-Player Sixes** (also called **Hollywood** or **Round Robin**) — A rotating partnership game where four players pair off for three separate 6-hole matches, with partnerships changing between each segment. Uses best-ball (foursome) match play scoring.

2. **3-Player Split Sixes** (also called **6-Point Game**, **English**, or **Cricket**) — A points-based individual competition for three players where 6 points are distributed per hole based on scoring position.

Both are popular, but they're completely different formats. The 4-player version dominates in the U.S.; the 3-player version is most popular in Europe/Australia but is gaining traction globally. Golfers often assume "Sixes" means the 4-player version unless specified otherwise.

## Game 1: 4-Player Sixes (Hollywood / Round Robin)

### Names and Terminology

**Primary names (in order of usage frequency):**
- **Sixes** — Most common in the U.S., especially casual/betting contexts
- **Round Robin** — Common in tournament/organized play contexts
- **Hollywood** — Colloquial, particularly in certain regions; used interchangeably with Sixes
- **6-6-6** — Descriptive name (three 6-hole segments)

**Regional variations:** The game is universal among English-speaking golfers, with no significant regional naming variations. "Hollywood" appears more in golf media and writing, while "Sixes" dominates casual conversation.

**Common confusion:** New players often confuse Sixes with Nassau (which is a wagering structure, not a partnership rotation). Sixes adds strategic complexity to standard foursome match play by forcing players to adapt to new partners mid-round.

Sources: [Golf.com](https://golf.com/news/money-games-explained-how-to-play-sixes/), [GolfDigest](https://www.golfdigest.com/story/how-to-play-sixes-golf-games-explained), [GolfCompendium](https://www.golfcompendium.com/2023/08/round-robin.html)

### Partnership Structure and Rotation

**Exact rotation pattern (18-hole round):**

- **Holes 1-6:** Player A & B vs. Player C & D
- **Holes 7-12:** Player A & C vs. Player B & D
- **Holes 13-18:** Player A & D vs. Player B & C

Each player pairs with each other player exactly once across the full 18 holes. This ensures balance and gives every player a chance to form different team dynamics.

**How partnerships are determined:**

- **In casual/betting play:** The four players usually establish partnerships on the first tee, either by mutual agreement or by having the course/group default (A & B typically play together for the first match).
- **In organized tournaments:** Pairings are set in advance by the tournament committee.
- **Rotating partners (not standard):** Most sources indicate that partnerships are fixed for each 6-hole block and predetermined on hole 1. Some groups may allow flexibility, but this is not the standard assumption.

**Key strategic point:** One player doesn't have a consistent "best" partner. A weaker golfer will be paired with different skill levels and playing styles, creating unpredictability.

Sources: [BEEZER Golf](https://beezergolf.com/golf-betting-games/round-robin-game-rules), [Golf.com](https://golf.com/news/money-games-explained-how-to-play-sixes/), [GolfCompendium](https://www.golfcompendium.com/2019/01/sixes-golf-format.html)

### Scoring and Match Play Format

**Primary scoring format:** **Fourball Best Ball (match play)**

- Each team uses the better of its two scores on each hole
- A hole is won by the team whose best score is lower than the opposing team's best score
- The team winning the hole gets 1 point
- Ties on a hole result in each team getting 0.5 points (or split the point) — though this is less common in casual betting play
- **Tiebreaker within a match:** If the low scores are equal, the low score of the second player breaks the tie (e.g., a 3&4 beats a 3&5)

**Scoring terminology:**
- Each 6-hole segment is a separate "match" or "bet"
- At the end of 18 holes, each player has won/lost three separate matches (one per segment)
- Final settlement is based on net wins minus losses across all three matches

**Alternative scoring options mentioned:**

1. **Total stroke play (less common):** Sum the total strokes for both players in each 6-hole match; lower total wins the match. This removes the best-ball advantage and emphasizes overall consistency.

2. **Stableford (less common):** Award points based on performance relative to par on each hole. Birdies worth more than pars, etc. This encourages aggressive play but is rarely used in casual Sixes.

3. **Points per hole (variation):** Award 1 point to the team that wins each hole, then award extra points for winning multiple holes within a 6-hole segment (e.g., 2 bonus points for winning 5+ holes in a segment).

**"Umbrella" bonus:** If one team wins all 6 holes in a segment, the points are doubled (so a 6-0 hole win becomes 12 points). This is not universal and is a house rule variation — sources don't indicate if this applies only in certain stakes or betting contexts.

Sources: [Golf.com](https://golf.com/news/money-games-explained-how-to-play-sixes/), [BEEZER Golf](https://beezergolf.com/golf-betting-games/sixes-game-rules), [18Birdies](https://18birdies.com/clubhouse/golf-games/golf-games-how-to-play-the-sixes-golf-game)

### Handicapping and Net vs. Gross Play

**Net play (most common in casual betting):**
- Lowest handicapper plays scratch (0 course handicap)
- Other players get the difference between their course handicap and the scratch player's (e.g., if A is 10-handicap and B is 25-handicap, B gets 15 strokes)
- Strokes are applied per hole based on the stroke allocation on the scorecard
- A team wins by low net score

**Gross play:**
- All scores count as-is, no handicap adjustment
- Typically used in organized tournaments where handicaps would create unfair advantage
- Less common in casual betting games

**Why handicapping matters:** Without handicaps, a foursome with mixed abilities (e.g., scratch golfer + 25-handicapper) would be very unbalanced. Handicapping "levels the field" so the weaker players have a legitimate chance to win and win money.

Sources: [BEEZER Golf](https://beezergolf.com/golf-betting-games/sixes-game-rules), [GolfLink](https://www.golflink.com/lifestyle/sixes-golf-game-rules-strategies-snatch-cash)

### Pressing Rules

**Pressing in Sixes is structured like Nassau pressing:**

- A team that is **losing** a 6-hole match can initiate a **"press"** on any hole within that segment (typically after hole 3, so there are still 3 holes to play).
- If the pressed team accepts the press, **a new match begins from that hole** with its own separate wager.
- **Example:** In the holes 1-6 match, if Team A goes 0-3 down (lost holes 1, 2, 3), they can press on hole 4. If accepted, holes 4-6 become a new, separate match with its own bet. The original holes 1-3 match stands independently.
- **Press matches can be pressed:** If the press match itself goes 2 down, it can be pressed again, creating a "sub-press" with its own distinct wager and outcome.
- **Nassau-style Sixes variation:** Some groups play Sixes in Nassau format, which breaks each 6-hole segment into three 3-hole mini-matches (front 3, back 3, overall 6). This creates more pressing opportunities.

**Betting mechanics:**
- Each match/press is a separate wager, settled independently
- At the end of 18 holes, a player calculates net wins minus losses across all matches (original + presses) and pays/receives the difference
- **Example:** Player 1 wins match 1 (+$5), loses match 2 (-$5), loses press in match 2 (-$5), wins match 3 (+$5) = $0 net (wins and losses offset)

**Pressing is optional:** Presses are only valid if the other team accepts them. In casual play, presses are almost always accepted to keep the game competitive and fun.

Sources: [BEEZER Golf](https://beezergolf.com/golf-betting-games/sixes-game-rules), [Golf.com](https://golf.com/news/money-games-explained-how-to-play-sixes/), [GolfLink](https://www.golflink.com/lifestyle/sixes-golf-game-rules-strategies-snatch-cash)

### Betting and Stakes

**Typical stake structure:**
- **Per-match betting:** Most common — a fixed dollar amount per 6-hole match (e.g., $5 per match)
- **Example:** If you play Sixes for $5/match, your maximum loss in a single match is $5. Over three matches, max loss is $15. With presses, exposure can increase.
- **Per-point betting (less common):** Some groups bet per hole/point; less common because it's harder to track mid-round

**Stake amounts (from sources):** Not standardized; depends on group. Examples mentioned include $1, $5, $10, $25 per match. Stakes are entirely player-determined.

**Settlement:**
- Pay out difference at the end of 18 holes
- Example: Player A wins matches 1 & 3 ($5 each = +$10), loses match 2 (-$5) = net +$5 owed
- All presses are settled independently

**Why Sixes appeals to mixed-ability groups:** Because each player partners with each other player, weak players aren't stuck carrying the full round (as in foursome alternate shot) or paired with bad partners (as in consistent partnerships). This makes stakes feel fair even in uneven groups.

Sources: [Golf.com](https://golf.com/news/money-games-explained-how-to-play-sixes/), [BEEZER Golf](https://beezergolf.com/golf-betting-games/sixes-game-rules)

### Common Variations

**Nassau-style Sixes:**
- Each 6-hole match is subdivided into three 3-hole matches: "front" (holes 1-3 of match), "back" (holes 4-6 of match), and "overall" (all 6 holes)
- Creates more betting opportunities and more pressing scenarios
- Example: Holes 1-6 match has three bets (front, back, overall), holes 7-12 match has three bets, holes 13-18 match has three bets = 9 total bets per round instead of 3
- Much more complex than standard Sixes; popular in serious/high-stakes games

**Progressive stakes:**
- Increase the wager with each 6-hole match (e.g., match 1 = $5, match 2 = $10, match 3 = $15)
- Adds mounting pressure and risk as the round progresses
- Less common in casual play

**Best ball vs. individual score variations (less common):**
- Standard Sixes uses best ball (lower of two team scores)
- Some groups use "both scores count" — each player's score matters separately, not just the best (this is non-standard and changes the game significantly)

**Scoring method combinations:**
- Sixes can be combined with other formats (scramble, alternate shot, Vegas) in the partnership structure, though this is rare in casual betting play
- Focus remains on the partnership rotation; the within-partnership scoring rules vary

**Junk/side bets layered on top:**
- Greenies: 1 point for hitting closest to the pin on a par 3 (optional, separate from main Sixes bet)
- Sandies: Points for up & down from bunker
- Birdies: Points for birdie or eagle
- Dots: Points for fewest putts on a hole
- These are entirely optional and secondary to the main Sixes wager

Sources: [Golf.com](https://golf.com/news/money-games-explained-how-to-play-sixes/), [GolfLink](https://www.golflink.com/lifestyle/sixes-golf-game-rules-strategies-snatch-cash), [GolfCompendium](https://www.golfcompendium.com/2019/01/sixes-golf-format.html)

### Strategy and Tips

**1. Adapt to your partner quickly**
- You have only 6 holes to establish rhythm with each new partner
- Observe their tendencies on the first 1-2 holes (do they play conservatively? attack pins aggressively? miss short or long?)
- Once you understand their style, complement it (if they're confident, let them lead; if they're struggling, take the pressure off)

**2. Keep yourself in every hole**
- In best-ball, you're "the insurance policy" for your partner
- If your partner is in trouble, your goal is to hit a safe, playable shot that gives the team a chance
- Don't try to be a hero or make up strokes with a risky shot if you're already in trouble — a conservative bogey scores 1 point, an aggressive double scores 0

**3. Manage putting psychology**
- The player with the longest putt controls the momentum of the hole
- If you have a makeable putt and your opponent has a longer putt, make yours first — it puts pressure on them and swings psychological advantage
- Leaving putts short eliminates any chance of holing a momentum-swinging long putt

**4. Know when to be aggressive vs. conservative**
- If both opponents are in trouble, don't take unnecessary risk to win the hole — a par is a likely win
- If an opponent has a good position, you might need to attack to win the hole
- Best ball rewards discipline; you don't need both team members to play perfectly

**5. Understand press strategy**
- When you're down in a match, pressing gives you a second chance, but only if you're confident you can win the remaining holes
- Conversely, if you're ahead and a team presses, evaluate if you think you'll win the pressed match before accepting (in casual play, you usually accept to keep it competitive)

**6. Be honest about skill distribution**
- If your group has mixed abilities, handicaps are essential — otherwise the good players will win all three matches
- Without handicaps, Sixes doesn't work well in mixed-ability foursomes (it becomes a "pros vs. amateurs" game)

Sources: [GolfLink](https://www.golflink.com/lifestyle/sixes-golf-game-rules-strategies-snatch-cash), [BEEZER Golf](https://beezergolf.com/golf-betting-games/sixes-game-rules), [Tampa Bay Downs](https://www.tampabaydowns.com/a-guide-to-the-sixes-golf-game/)

### Common Mistakes New Players Make

**1. Being too aggressive when it's not necessary**
- Trying to win every hole instead of focusing on "don't lose the hole"
- A safe bogey that scores 1 point is better than a risky recovery that turns into a double (0 points)
- New players overestimate the value of aggressive play in best ball

**2. Not adapting to partnership changes fast enough**
- Assuming your new partner plays like your previous partner (they don't)
- Not reading how your partner responds to pressure in the first 1-2 holes
- Takes 2-3 holes to really sync with a new partner; by then, you're halfway through the match

**3. Placing blame on the partner for losses**
- In best ball, if your partner shoots 7 and you shoot 8, the 7 counts — you don't have a say
- New players focus on their own score instead of supporting their partner's shot
- Best ball is about accepting that your partner's score matters more than yours (it's the lower one that counts)

**4. Not understanding the stake structure**
- Assuming you can lose unlimited money with presses (presses are optional and limited by agreement)
- Not tracking wins/losses across matches correctly
- Miscalculating net settlement at the end

**5. Overcomplicating the game**
- Too many house rules introduced at once confuses scoring
- New players benefit from playing "pure" Sixes (no umbrella, no Junk bets, no Nassau variation) first

Sources: [GolfLink](https://www.golflink.com/lifestyle/sixes-golf-game-rules-strategies-snatch-cash), [Tampa Bay Downs](https://www.tampabaydowns.com/a-guide-to-the-sixes-golf-game/)

### Default Expectations (What "Let's Play Sixes" Means)

When someone says "Let's play Sixes" in a casual golf setting, golfers assume:

1. ✓ **Four players** — exactly 4, not 3, not 5
2. ✓ **Fourball best ball** — use the lower of two team scores per hole
3. ✓ **Three 6-hole matches** — holes 1-6, 7-12, 13-18, each scored independently
4. ✓ **Predetermined partnerships** — decided on hole 1 and don't change except between segments
5. ✓ **Match play** — earn points for winning holes
6. ✓ **Net scoring** — handicaps applied (unless specifically stated "gross")
7. ✓ **Pressing allowed** — losing team can press a match mid-segment
8. ✓ **Standard partnerships rotation** — A&B vs C&D (1-6), A&C vs B&D (7-12), A&D vs B&C (13-18)
9. ✓ **Money game** — assumes some wagering, though the amount is negotiable ($1-$25/match is typical)
10. ✗ **No Nassau subdivision** — unless explicitly stated ("Let's play Sixes Nassau")
11. ✗ **No Junk bets** — unless explicitly stated ("Sixes with Greenies and Sandies")
12. ✗ **No umbrella rule** — unless specifically introduced by the group

Sources: [Golf.com](https://golf.com/news/money-games-explained-how-to-play-sixes/), [BEEZER Golf](https://beezergolf.com/golf-betting-games/sixes-game-rules), [GolfCompendium](https://www.golfcompendium.com/2023/08/round-robin.html)

## Game 2: 3-Player Split Sixes (6-Point Game / English / Cricket)

### Names and Terminology

**Primary names:**
- **Split Sixes** — Most common in U.S.
- **6-Point Game** — Descriptive name, popular in some regions
- **English** — Regional name (especially in UK/Commonwealth countries)
- **Cricket** — Older name, rarely used today but still understood
- **6-6-6** — Occasionally used to describe the structure (6 points on each hole, repeated for 18 holes or divided into three 6-hole segments)

**Regional variations:** Split Sixes is most popular in Australia, UK, and Europe. In the U.S., it's less common than the 4-player version but is growing in popularity as an option for threesomes.

**Common confusion:**
- Golfers often confuse Split Sixes with **Nines** (9-Point Game) — both are 3-player point games, but Split Sixes uses 6 points per hole, Nines uses 9 points per hole
- Some golfers confuse Split Sixes with the 4-player version; the 3-player version is fundamentally different (individuals vs. teams)
- Not related to the 4-player Sixes game except by name and the 6-hole structure some variations use

Sources: [LiveAbout](https://www.liveabout.com/split-sixes-1564267), [CaddieHQ](https://www.caddiehq.com/resources/what-is-split-sixes-in-golf), [GolfCompendium](https://www.golfcompendium.com/2024/04/split-sixes.html)

### How It Works: The 4-2-0 Point System

**Standard point distribution per hole:**
- **Low score:** 4 points
- **Middle score:** 2 points
- **High score:** 0 points
- **Total per hole:** 6 points (always distributed completely among the three players)

**Why 4-2-0?** It rewards the best score heavily (4 points) while still giving the middle scorer some credit (2 points). The high scorer gets nothing, which creates incentive to stay competitive. The 4-2-0 system is the most balanced and is the standard (though not universal).

**Handling ties:**

When two or three players tie on a hole, the points are redistributed:

- **Two players tie for low:**
  - Each tied player: 3 points
  - High player: 0 points
  - Total: 6 points (3+3+0)

- **Two players tie for high (low score wins outright):**
  - Low player: 4 points
  - Each tied player: 1 point
  - Total: 6 points (4+1+1)

- **All three tie:**
  - Each player: 2 points
  - Total: 6 points (2+2+2)

**Key insight:** Even if you don't score the best, you're never completely shut out of points unless you're in last place alone.

Sources: [LiveAbout](https://www.liveabout.com/split-sixes-1564267), [CaddieHQ](https://www.caddiehq.com/resources/how-to-play-split-sixes-in-golf), [GolfCompendium](https://www.golfcompendium.com/2024/04/split-sixes.html)

### Game Structure: Per-Hole vs. Per-Segment

**Per-hole structure (most common):**
- Each of the 18 holes is worth 6 points
- Players accumulate points hole-by-hole over the full 18
- Winner is the player with the most points at the end of 18 holes
- **Total points at stake:** 108 points (18 holes × 6 points)

**Per-segment structure (alternative):**
- The 18-hole round is divided into three 6-hole matches
- Holes 1-6 = Match 1, Holes 7-12 = Match 2, Holes 13-18 = Match 3
- Points are tallied separately for each match; the player with the most points in a segment wins that match
- Scoreboard resets after each 6-hole segment
- This structure mirrors the 4-player Sixes format and creates a "three mini-matches" feel
- **Less common** than per-hole structure in casual play

**When per-segment is used:** Primarily when groups want to create separate betting opportunities (bet $5/match) or when they want to introduce pressing (similar to 4-player Sixes). This adds complexity.

Sources: [LiveAbout](https://www.liveabout.com/split-sixes-1564267), [GolfCompendium](https://www.golfcompendium.com/2024/04/split-sixes.html)

### Split Sixes vs. Nines (9-Point Game): The Difference

Both are 3-player golf betting games, but they use different point totals per hole:

| Aspect | Split Sixes (6-Point) | Nines (9-Point) |
|--------|----------------------|-----------------|
| **Low score** | 4 points | 5 points |
| **Middle score** | 2 points | 3 points |
| **High score** | 0 points | 1 point |
| **Total per hole** | 6 points | 9 points |
| **Total 18 holes** | 108 points | 162 points |
| **When to use** | Faster point swings; winner more decisive | Tighter scoring; high score still valuable; less variance |

**Key difference:** In Nines, even the high scorer earns 1 point (always in the game). In Split Sixes, the high scorer gets nothing (more punitive). Nines is "friendlier" to weaker players; Split Sixes is more competitive/aggressive.

**Popularity:** Split Sixes is more popular than Nines in most regions, likely because it's more "forgiving" (middle score still earns 2 points) while still being decisive (high score earns 0).

Sources: [LiveAbout](https://www.liveabout.com/play-the-nine-points-golf-game-1564224), [GolfLink](https://www.golflink.com/lifestyle/best-games-threesome)

### Betting and Stakes

**Typical structure:**
- **Per-point betting:** Most common — agree on a dollar value per point (e.g., $0.25/point, $1/point)
- **Example:** If you have 45 points and the leader has 50 points, you owe them $5 (for the 5-point difference at $1/point)
- **Per-match betting (if using 6-hole segments):** Like 4-player Sixes, bet per 6-hole match (e.g., $5/match)

**Bet amounts:** Typically smaller per-point amounts ($0.10-$1) because there are many point swings. Over 18 holes with 6 points per hole, point differences can be substantial.

**Settlement:**
- Tally points at the end of 18 holes (or after each match if using 6-hole segments)
- Player with the most points wins
- Other two players owe the difference
- Example: Player A = 55 points, Player B = 50 points, Player C = 45 points
  - At $1/point: A receives $5 from B and $10 from C (total +$15)
  - B receives $5 from C (net -$5 to A, +$5 from C)
  - C pays $10 to A and $5 to B (net -$15)

Sources: [LiveAbout](https://www.liveabout.com/split-sixes-1564267), [CaddieHQ](https://www.caddiehq.com/resources/how-to-play-split-sixes-in-golf)

### Strategy and Tips for Split Sixes

**1. Focus on consistent scoring, not just winning holes**
- In a points game, you earn points even when you don't win the hole (unless you're in last place)
- A middle score (2 points) repeated across 18 holes = 36 points
- Consistent play beats aggressive play; reliability wins Split Sixes

**2. Don't get discouraged by one bad hole**
- You don't lose points; you just earn fewer than your competitors on that hole
- A 7 on a hole where others shoot 6 and 8 still earns you 2 points (not 0)
- Unlike Skins or Match Play, there are no catastrophic "zeros"

**3. Manage pacing and pressure**
- Three players means faster play (no waiting for a 4th player to finish)
- Tight scoring creates pressure, especially on back nine
- Play your game, don't get caught up in what the others are doing

**4. Understand the 4-2-0 distribution psychology**
- High scorers have nothing to lose — they're already at 0, so they might play aggressively
- Middle scorers are incentivized to hold 2 points
- Low scorers want to protect their 4 points
- This creates interesting risk/reward dynamics

**5. Handicapping (if used)**
- Apply net scoring similar to 4-player Sixes to level the field
- Less common in casual play but essential if skill levels differ significantly

Sources: [LiveAbout](https://www.liveabout.com/split-sixes-1564267), [CaddieHQ](https://www.caddiehq.com/resources/what-is-split-sixes-in-golf)

### Common Mistakes in Split Sixes

**1. Playing too aggressively after a bad hole**
- Golfers try to "make up" points after a high-score hole by playing riskier
- This often leads to worse scores and more lost points
- Conservative recovery is better than aggressive recovery in a 6-point system

**2. Not understanding that you don't need to win holes to win the game**
- New players think they need to win 10+ holes to win (incorrect)
- A consistent 2-3 point average per hole can win the game
- If you score middle 12 holes + low 3 holes + high 3 holes = 12×2 + 3×4 + 3×0 = 36 points (competitive, not dominant)

**3. Overanalyzing other players' scores mid-round**
- Split Sixes is about YOUR score vs. the field, not head-to-head match
- Worrying about what others are scoring distracts from your own play
- Focus on executing your own shot, let the points sort themselves out

**4. Not tracking points correctly**
- With 6 points per hole, 18 holes, it's easy to lose track
- Recommend one person keep a running tally visible to all three players
- Prevents disputes and keeps everyone engaged

**5. Playing too conservatively**
- Some golfers are so focused on avoiding the high score that they leave themselves no margin
- Being in middle = 2 points; don't be afraid to attack for low if the situation allows
- Balance is key

Sources: [GolfCompendium](https://www.golfcompendium.com/2024/04/split-sixes.html), [LiveAbout](https://www.liveabout.com/split-sixes-1564267)

### Split Sixes Variations

**1. Carryover rule (house rule):**
- If all three players tie on a hole (all score the same), no points are awarded
- Those 6 points "carry over" to the next hole
- Next hole is now worth 12 points (6+6), creating a dramatic swing
- Adds drama but also adds variance/luck; less common in friendly games

**2. Per-match structure (mentioned earlier):**
- Divide into three 6-hole matches
- Reset scoreboard after each match
- Allows for separate bets and pressing (like 4-player Sixes)
- Creates three "tournaments within the tournament"

**3. Best ball vs. individual (non-standard):**
- Standard Split Sixes = individual scores for all three
- Some groups might pair two against one, but this removes the "split" nature of the game and is very non-standard

**4. Bonus bets / Junk:**
- Greenies, Sandies, Birdies, Dots added on top of 6-point pot
- Less common in Split Sixes than in 4-player Sixes but possible

Sources: [GolfCompendium](https://www.golfcompendium.com/2024/04/split-sixes.html), [CaddieHQ](https://www.caddiehq.com/resources/what-is-split-sixes-in-golf)

### Default Expectations (What "Let's Play Split Sixes" Means)

When someone says "Let's play Split Sixes" in a casual setting with three golfers:

1. ✓ **Three players** — exactly 3, not 4
2. ✓ **Individual competition** — each player's score counts separately; no partnerships
3. ✓ **Per-hole scoring** — 6 points awarded each hole, 18 holes total = 108 points max
4. ✓ **4-2-0 point distribution** — low=4, middle=2, high=0
5. ✓ **Gross scoring** — individual scores count as-is (unless net scoring is pre-agreed)
6. ✓ **Full 18-hole round** — not divided into segments (unless pre-agreed)
7. ✓ **Money game** — assumes some wagering, typically per-point basis ($0.25-$1/point)
8. ✓ **Ties split points** — all tie rules apply (2-way tie, 3-way tie)
9. ✗ **No pressing** — unlike 4-player Sixes; pressing doesn't make sense in individual play
10. ✗ **No 6-hole segment format** — unless explicitly stated ("Split Sixes with 6-hole matches")
11. ✗ **No Junk bets** — unless explicitly agreed
12. ✗ **No Nines variant** — Split Sixes specifically means 6-point system, not 9-point

## Key Findings & Content Gaps

### Major Distinction: Two Games with One Name
The research reveals that "Sixes" is genuinely confusing because it's two completely different games:
1. **4-Player Sixes** (partnership rotation, team match play) — Common in the U.S., casual betting
2. **3-Player Split Sixes** (individual points competition) — More popular in Europe/Australia, growing in the U.S.

**Content implication:** Stick will likely need TWO separate guides (or one detailed guide with clear sections) because:
- Mechanics are completely different (team vs. individual, match play vs. points)
- Strategy is completely different
- Handicapping approach differs
- Betting structures differ
- Default expectations differ

### Nomenclature Challenge
Golfers use multiple names interchangeably for the 4-player version:
- "Sixes" = default/most common
- "Round Robin" = also very common
- "Hollywood" = colloquial but well-understood

**Content implication:** The guide title should probably be "Sixes (4-Player) and Split Sixes (3-Player)" to disambiguate from the start. Or separate them into two guides.

### Absence of "Standard" Pressing in 3-Player
3-Player Split Sixes doesn't support pressing because there are no teams — pressing is a team negotiation/bet. This is a fundamental difference.

**Content implication:** This needs to be emphasized in the Split Sixes section to avoid confusion with the 4-player version.

### Handicapping Universally Recommended for Mixed Abilities
Both variants assume handicapping for casual play when skill levels differ. Without handicaps, games become unfair if one player is significantly stronger.

**Content implication:** Every guide should emphasize the handicap option and include an explanation of how to apply course handicaps.

### Split Sixes vs. Nines Confusion
Golfers frequently confuse these two 3-player games. The primary difference is 6 points vs. 9 points per hole. Split Sixes is more punitive (high scorer = 0 points); Nines is more inclusive (high scorer = 1 point).

**Content implication:** If writing Split Sixes content, include a section explaining how it differs from Nines and when to choose one over the other.

### Settlement Complexity
Both games require careful tracking of wins/losses (4-player) or points (3-player). New players often miscalculate final settlement.

**Content implication:** The guides should include worked settlement examples with specific names and numbers. This is where Stick's app advantage is highest — automated settlement tracking.

### House Rules Are Rampant
Both games have numerous optional house rules (umbrella, carryover, Nassau format, Junk bets, progressive stakes). Standardization is weak.

**Content implication:** The guides should clarify "this is the standard rule" vs. "this is a common house rule variation." Helps readers know what to expect and when to negotiate.

### Competitor Coverage Is Shallow
18Birdies and BEEZER Golf both support Sixes, but their content/guides on the site are brief (200-400 words). Opportunity exists for Stick to build 1500-2500 word in-depth guides that competitors don't offer.
