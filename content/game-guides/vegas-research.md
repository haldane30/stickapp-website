# Vegas Golf Game: Real-World Research

**Date:** March 1, 2026
**Research Scope:** Golf communities (Reddit, GolfWRX, Golf Digest, The Fried Egg, GolfWRX forums, golf blogs)
**Purpose:** Understand how golfers actually play Vegas, regional variations, common disputes, and what competitors support

---

## 1. How Most Groups Actually Play Vegas

### Four-Player Format (Standard)

Four golfers split into two teams of two. Teams are determined on the first tee (typically by throwing balls or a tee) and stay together for the entire round.

**Core Scoring Rule:**
- Each team's score is "paired" (not added). If Golfer A shoots 4 and Golfer B shoots 5, the team score is "45" (low number first).
- The difference between team scores determines points won/lost that hole.
- Example: Team 1 shoots 44, Team 2 shoots 66. Team 1 wins 22 points (66 - 44 = 22).

**Most Common House Rules in Casual Play:**

1. **The 10+ Rule** (Nearly universal)
   - When any player scores 10 or higher on a hole, that high score goes first to prevent massive swings
   - Example: Player shoots 10, partner shoots 5 → Score becomes 105 instead of 510
   - This protects the game from becoming unplayable on bad holes

2. **Flipping the Bird** (Very common)
   - When a team makes a birdie, they can flip the other team's score (high number first)
   - Example: You birdie while opponents score 48 → You flip it to 84
   - Flip is nullified if both teams have at least one birdie on the same hole
   - Creates tension and momentum swings

3. **Carryover** (Common but optional)
   - If teams tie multiple holes in a row, when one team wins the next hole, points are multiplied
   - Example: Teams tie holes 1, 2, and 3. Team A wins hole 4 by 10 points → 10 × 3 = 30 points
   - Significantly amplifies the game; must be agreed upon before play starts
   - Some groups treat this as standard, others don't use it at all

4. **Birdie/Eagle Multipliers** (Moderate adoption)
   - Lone birdie by winning team doubles the points
   - Eagle by winning team triples the points
   - Adds another layer of pressure and reward

5. **Gross vs. Net**
   - Most casual groups play gross (no handicap adjustment)
   - Groups with mixed skill levels apply handicap differentials: the higher-handicap team gets strokes equal to the difference in combined handicaps
   - Less common to play full net scores; most use gross or handicap-adjusted gross

### Fixed vs. Dynamic Teams

Research shows most groups play **fixed teams** (same partners for all 18 holes). This is how it's played in nearly all casual foursomes.

Dynamic/rotating partnership systems (like "Amigos") exist but are much less common and typically reserved for larger groups or specific tournament formats.

## 2. Regional Variations

### Daytona (Not Just a Name – It's Different)

Daytona is a distinct Vegas variant, not just a regional name.

**Key Difference from Vegas:**
- In Vegas: Low score always goes first (except 10+ rule)
- In Daytona: If one team scores par or better, the low number comes first. If both are over par, the high number comes first
- Example: Scores of 4 and 7 → Team scores 47 (par kept low score first). Scores of 5 and 6 → Team scores 65 (both over par, high first)

**Adoption:** Less common than standard Vegas but recognized across golf communities as a legitimate variant

### Amigos (Completely Different Game, Not a Vegas Variant)

Despite being mentioned alongside Vegas, Amigos is a separate game with a fundamentally different structure:

**How It Works:**
- Played with 3, 4, 5, 6+ players
- Players rotate partners based on fairway position (same side = partners for that hole)
- Partners change every hole; not fixed teams
- Scoring: Players on the ladder pay/receive differential amounts (no concatenation like Vegas)

**Adoption:** Niche; used mostly when groups have more than 4 players and want everyone to have equal play time

### Other Regional Names

Research did not reveal other widely-used regional names for Vegas outside of "Daytona." Vegas appears to be called "Las Vegas" or "Vegas" consistently across most golf communities.

## 3. Common Disputes and Confusion

### The 10+ Rule (Most Disputed)

**The Question:** When does it apply? Does it apply to 9s? What about a 10 vs. an 8?

- Standard interpretation: Any score 10 or higher → that score goes first
- Some groups debate whether a 9 triggers the rule (most say no, only 10+)
- When both players on a team score 10+, convention is still unclear in some groups (most use low number first between the 10s)

### Flip Mechanics

**Common Disputes:**
1. "Can you flip after your partner already played the next hole?" (No, flip must happen immediately when birdie is made)
2. "Does a flip cancel out a previous flip from the other team?" (No; each flip stands independently per hole)
3. "If both teams birdie, do we flip back and forth?" (No; if both teams have at least one birdie, flip is blocked entirely for that hole)

**Confusion:** How the flip interacts with carryover (does a flip'd score use the carry multiplier? Most say yes – flip happens first, then multiply)

### Tie Scenarios

**Question:** What happens if teams score the same on a hole?
- Standard: Zero points awarded; hole is a tie
- But if carryover is in play, that tie counts toward the carry total
- Some casual groups don't track ties clearly, leading to disputes later in the round

### Carryover Point Values

**Dispute:** Do you multiply by the number of ties (2, 3, 4) or the hole number (hole #5)?
- Most common: Multiply by the number of tied holes (if 2 holes tie, multiply next winning hole by 2)
- Less common: Some groups track the highest carry and reset it after it's used
- High confusion in groups that don't establish the rule upfront

## 4. Three-Player Adaptations

### Method 1: Doubles Their Score (Most Common)

One player plays alone; their score is counted twice to form a team score.

- Example: Solo player scores 5 → Team score is 55
- Solo player scores birdie (3) → Team score is 33 (and can flip the other team's score)
- **Advantage:** Simple to understand and calculate
- **Disadvantage:** Solo player is heavily disadvantaged unless very skilled

### Method 2: Rotating Ghost Player

Rotate partnerships every six holes; the "ghost" player (scratch golfer, always makes par) stays consistent:

- Example: Holes 1-6: Players A & B vs. Ghost. Holes 7-12: Players A & C vs. Ghost.
- Ghost always scores par (no birdies, no bogeys)
- **Advantage:** More balanced; everyone gets 12 holes of team play
- **Disadvantage:** Ghost player isn't real (can't pressure or contribute emotionally)

### Method 3: Two vs. One (Less Common)

Two players team together against the solo player, but solo player's score is doubled:

- Slightly different psychology; some feel it's fairer
- Less common than the doubles-their-score method

**Key Finding:** Three-player Vegas is widely acknowledged as not ideal, but the "doubles their score" method is by far the most used because it's simplest.

## 5. Point Values and Betting Amounts

### Casual Play (Saturday Foursome)

- **$0.25 per point** – Very casual, drinks-focused
- **$0.50 per point** – Most common casual bet; creates some stakes without serious money
- **$1.00 per point** – Moderate stakes; typical max loss/win per hole is $18-22 (18-22 point swings)

### High Stakes / Serious Play

- **$2-5 per point** – High stakes; serious golfers only
- **Golf trip/vacation money** – Some groups pool larger bets for tournament-style play

### Point Distribution Examples

- **Common swing per hole:** 8-22 points (when scores vary by 0.8-2.2 point difference in pairs)
- **Big swing:** 44 vs. 99 = 55 points (if no 10+ rule) or capped at maximum house rules allow
- **Zero swing:** Both teams tie (44 vs. 44 = no points awarded)

**Pain Point:** Without app tracking, mental math errors are common when multiplying points by wagered amount across 18 holes. Most golfers want automatic settlement calculation.

## 6. Handicap Usage

### Gross Play (Most Common)

Majority of casual groups play **gross** (no handicap adjustment). It's simpler and doesn't require knowing everyone's handicap.

### Handicap-Adjusted Play (Less Common but Used)

When skill levels vary significantly:

- **Team handicap differential method:** Higher-handicap team gets strokes equal to the difference
  - Example: Team A has 12+14=26 combined handicap. Team B has 8+6=14. Team A gets 12 strokes for the round (26-14=12).
  - Strokes are distributed by hole (number 1 handicap holes get the strokes first)
- **Net scores per hole:** Some groups convert to net scores before pairing
  - Less common because it adds complexity

### Mixed Skill Levels

Research shows that handicap adjustment is **optional** and many groups simply accept that better golfers will win more often. The "flip the bird" rule and carryover are used to keep games competitive even without handicap adjustments.

**Key Finding:** Handicap usage is not standard; groups decide case-by-case based on skill spread.

## 7. Press Rules

### Basic Press Mechanic

When a team is down in the match, they can "press" to reset the current match and start a new one running concurrently:

- Example: Team A is down 50 points through hole 10. They press. Now there's the original match (holes 1-18) and a new press match (holes 11-18).
- If the new press is won by the pressing team, they win only the press amount (not the whole match).

### Press in Vegas Specifically

**Less Documented Than in Match Play, But Used:**

- Press rules vary widely by region and group
- Some Vegas players use single "double-down" mechanics (you can double the entire remaining bet once)
- Others use traditional press (multiple presses allowed, each starts a new concurrent match)
- Some groups don't allow presses in Vegas at all (prefer the natural carryover swings)

**Key Finding:** Press usage in Vegas is inconsistent and less standardized than in Match Play or Skins. Most casual groups don't use presses; they rely on carryover instead.

## 8. Monte Carlo

### How It Works

Instead of pairing scores, multiply them:

- Vegas example: 4 × 5 = 20 (vs. Vegas 45)
- Difference: Team 1 shoots 4×5=20, Team 2 shoots 6×7=42. Team 1 wins 22 points (42-20=22)

### Volatility

Monte Carlo is **significantly more volatile** than Vegas because high scores get amplified:

- Bad hole example: Partner shoots 9, you shoot 5 → Monte Carlo score is 45 points vs. Vegas score of 59
- Even worse example: Partner shoots 10, you shoot 8 → Monte Carlo is 80 points vs. Vegas 108 (but 10+ rule would make Vegas 810)

### Adoption

**Niche variant.** Monte Carlo is recognized and played, but rarely. Most research mentions it as a variation Vegas players *can* try, not something they commonly do.

**Use Case:** Groups looking to add extreme volatility for a specific round (like a trip final match).

## 10. Common Complaints and Pain Points

### Scoring Complexity

**What Golfers Complain About:**
1. **Mental math errors** – Pairing numbers is harder than adding; easy to misread 45 as 54
2. **The 10+ rule creates confusion** – Forgetting when to apply it; is it 9+? 10+? What if one player scores 10 and the other scores 8?
3. **Carryover multipliers** – Groups forget what the carry count is midway through the round

**What They Want:** App that handles all pairing, special rules, and settlement automatically

### Settlement Friction

**Pain Point:** Money settlement at the end of round
- Manual calculation of 18 holes × per-point wager = easy errors
- Awkward moments when someone disagrees on final tally
- No paper record (most groups track mentally)

**What They Want:** Automatic final settlement with per-player breakdown

### Rule Disagreements Pre-Game

**Pain Point:** Every group plays Vegas slightly differently
- Does your group use flip? Carryover? Multipliers? 10+ rule on 9s?
- New players joining a group don't know house rules
- Same group plays different rules on different days (inconsistent memory)

**What They Want:** Clear preset rule options or ability to define house rules at game start

### Three-Player Play

**Pain Point:** Vegas is optimized for 4 players
- With 3, the solo player (doubled score) is heavily disadvantaged mathematically
- Groups debate whether to use ghost player or just accept imbalance
- No standard adopted by most communities

**What They Want:** A balanced three-player mode that doesn't handicap one player

### Carryover Lack of Documentation

**Pain Point:** Almost no golf betting apps track carryover automatically
- 18Birdies and Beezer don't mention it
- Groups that use carryover track it manually (error-prone)
- Major reason some groups have stopped using carryover (too much mental overhead)

**What They Want:** Built-in carryover tracking with multiplier display

### Press Mechanics Confusion

**Pain Point:** Press rules are inconsistent; apps don't support them well in Vegas context
- Skins apps handle presses well; Vegas apps don't
- Groups often skip presses in Vegas and use carryover instead

**What They Want:** Optional press tracking if they want to use it

## 11. What Makes Vegas Stand Out vs. Other Games

### vs. Nassau

- **Nassau:** Broken into three separate matches (front 9, back 9, full 18); focuses on course momentum
- **Vegas:** Cumulative points per hole; high volatility possible on any single hole
- **Vegas appeal:** "Every shot matters because one birdie or bad hole can swing big points"

### vs. Skins

- **Skins:** Only the hole winner gets points (no points on ties)
- **Vegas:** Every hole generates points (even by small margins); no "dead" holes
- **Vegas appeal:** More continuous action; you can make a comeback from any position

### vs. Match Play

- **Match Play:** Track holes won/lost; first to win majority of holes wins
- **Vegas:** Track numerical point differential; accumulative
- **Vegas appeal:** More dramatic swings possible; higher point volatility

### vs. Junk (Dots)

- **Junk:** Points awarded for birdies, pars, bogeys (fixed value system)
- **Vegas:** Points awarded based on comparative performance (pair difference)
- **Vegas appeal:** "Better players get rewarded more heavily"

## 12. Key Insights for Product (Stick Vegas Game Design)

### Must-Have Features

1. **Automatic pairing** with 10+ rule built in (low number first, except 10+)
2. **Flip tracking** – visual indicator when birdie-flip occurs
3. **Optional carryover** – automatic multiplier if enabled at game start
4. **Three-player mode** – doubled score method (most expected)
5. **Handicap-adjusted option** – for mixed-skill groups
6. **Real-time settlement calculation** – total per-player by end of round

### Nice-to-Have Features

1. **Birdie/Eagle multipliers** – optional rule toggle
2. **Press tracking** – if player base demands it (less common in Vegas)
3. **Daytona variant** – recognized alternative for players who want it
4. **Monte Carlo variant** – niche but traceable
5. **House rule presets** – "Conservative Vegas" (no flip, no carryover) vs. "High-Stakes Vegas" (flip, carryover, multipliers)

### Feature Gaps in Competitors

- **None track carryover automatically** – clear opportunity
- **None handle three-player Vegas cleanly** – clear opportunity
- **Press support in Vegas is weak** – lower priority (less used than in other games)
- **No house rule customization** – all assume standard rules (misses group variance)

## 13. Settlement Math Examples

### Example 1: Basic Vegas (No Extras)

Teams: AB vs. CD

| Hole | A | B | AB Score | C | D | CD Score | Points | Winner |
|------|---|---|----------|---|---|----------|--------|--------|
| 1 | 4 | 5 | 45 | 5 | 6 | 56 | 11 | AB |
| 2 | 5 | 5 | 55 | 4 | 5 | 45 | 10 | CD |
| 3 | 3 | 4 | 34 | 3 | 4 | 34 | 0 | Tie |

**Points held:** AB +11, CD -10, Tie 0 → AB +1 (after 3 holes)

### Example 2: Vegas with Flip (Team AB Makes Birdie on Hole 2)

Hole 2: A=5, B=5, AB=55. C=4, D=5, CD=45. C makes birdie.

- CD wins the hole (45 < 55), difference is 10
- But A made a birdie, so AB can flip CD's score: 45 → 54
- **New difference:** 55 vs. 54 = 1 point for AB (not 10 for CD)

### Example 3: Vegas with Carryover (Ties on Holes 4-5, Win on Hole 6)

- Hole 4: Both teams 45 → Tie (carry count = 1)
- Hole 5: Both teams 44 → Tie (carry count = 2)
- Hole 6: AB shoots 45, CD shoots 55 → AB wins by 10 points
- **With carryover:** 10 × 3 (number of holes tied including this one) = 30 points for AB

## 15. Recommended Reading for Game Guide Draft

When drafting the Stick Vegas game guide, prioritize:

1. **Settle the 10+ rule clearly** with a worked example
2. **Explain flip mechanics step by step** (most confused rule)
3. **Carryover is optional** – show how it amplifies game; make clear it's a house rule
4. **Three-player adaptation** – explain the doubled-score method and acknowledge it's a compromise
5. **Settlement math example** – full 18-hole sample to show how points accumulate
6. **Common house rules section** – show the most popular variants so readers can pick their style
7. **Handicap usage** – explain that most casual groups play gross, show optional handicap method

This research shows Vegas is **less standardized than Nassau or Skins**, with high regional/group variance. The guide needs to acknowledge this and help new players understand what's "core" (flip, 10+ rule) vs. what's optional (carryover, multipliers).
