## 1. Standard Wolf Rules (Quick Reference)

From [Golf Digest](https://www.golfdigest.com/story/golf-game-wolf-betting-gambling-explained):

- **Setup:** Foursome with rotating Wolf order. One player per hole is Wolf.
- **Core mechanic:** Wolf tees off (timing debated—see section 4), then must choose a partner before the next player tees off, OR go Lone Wolf.
- **Partnership:** Wolf + chosen partner vs. the other two (2v2).
- **Lone Wolf:** Wolf plays alone against the three others (1v3).
- **Scoring (standard):**
  - If team with Wolf wins: Wolf and partner get 2 points each
  - If non-Wolf team wins: They get 3 points each
  - If Wolf goes Lone and wins: 4 points
  - If Wolf goes Lone and loses: Loses 2 points per "sheep"

**Scoring note:** Some sources cite 1-2-3 point systems (1 pt for winning hole, 2 pts for winning with partner selection, 3 pts for non-Wolf team winning). Exact scoring is heavily customized by group.

## 2. Major Variations by Category

### A. Decision Timing / Partner Selection

**Blind Wolf**
- Wolf declares going Lone BEFORE anyone tees off
- Sees no shots before committing
- Higher risk, higher reward
- Point multiplier: Typically doubles the stake (4x payout vs 2x for regular Lone Wolf)
- Some sources cite Blind Wolf at 3x or even 8x multiplier vs. standard

**Forced Lone Wolf**
- Occurs if Wolf waits until third player has teed off without selecting a partner
- Then must play alone (forced into 1v3)
- Acts as a penalty for indecision / time management

**Pro Wolf** (related to Blind Wolf)
- Variant where Blind Wolf declaration results in 3:1 payout (mentioned by some sources as distinct from standard Blind Wolf)

**Partner Refusal / Spit / Divorce**
- Once Wolf picks a partner, that player can "spit" and refuse the partnership
- Player who spits plays alone against all three (including the Wolf)
- Bet doubles when this happens
- Example: Wolf picks strong partner expecting 2v2, but partner thinks they're stronger solo and "pigs" the Wolf
- Must happen before leaving the tee box (some sources say before next shot)
- No take-backs: Once next player tees off, Wolf can't go back and select a previous player

---

### B. Team Configuration Variations

**Scotch Wolf** (Alternate Shot)
- Teams formed same way as standard Wolf (2v2 or 1v3)
- BUT: After tee shots, play proceeds as ALTERNATE SHOT
- Better tee shot is chosen, then other player hits second shot, alternating from there
- Adds complexity: Choosing partner now depends on alternate-shot skills, not just tee shot quality
- Example: Strong driver but weak iron player vs. mediocre driver but excellent iron player
- Requires knowing teammates' alternate-shot capabilities

**Canadian Wolf**
- Teams play based on NET SCORE (handicap adjusted)
- Unlike standard Wolf (gross score comparison), Canadian uses Course Handicap to compare net scores
- Tee order determined by NET score: lowest net score from previous hole tees first; highest net becomes Wolf
- Wolf chooses partner after seeing tee shots
- Both team scores (high and low net) matter for some scoring variants
- Advantage: Keeps all players competitive even after bad holes

---

### C. Multiplier / Double-Down Variations

**Hammer** (or Re-Hammer / Progressive Hammer)
- Doubles the point value of any hole mid-play
- Can be called by either team at any time during play
- Must be accepted or rejected before next shot
- If rejected: Rejecting team loses the hole at previous point value (hole is "pushed")
- If accepted: Other team can immediately call Re-Hammer (doubling again)
- Rule: Can't hammer twice in a row (teams alternate hammer right)
- Creates dynamic stakes management within a hole
- Source: [No Laying Up](https://nolayingup.com/videos/the-mixed-bag/the-mixed-bag/wolf-hammer) describes this as "The Most Dangerous Game in Golf"

**Roll the Dice** (variation)
- Wolf can declare before tee shot to "roll the dice" on a hole
- Blind commitment multiplies points (similar to Blind Wolf but perhaps with different thresholds)
- Limited info available; appears regional/informal

---

### D. Final Holes (17-18) Special Rules

**Last Place Wolf**
- Player in last place gets to be Wolf on holes 17 and 18
- Gives trailing player chance to catch up
- Acts as a "comeback mechanic"

**Alternative: First Place Wolf on Final Holes**
- Leader gets Wolf on 17-18 instead
- Intensifies pressure on front-runner
- Less commonly played than Last Place Wolf

**High Score Wolf**
- Sometimes the player with the highest cumulative score gets 17-18
- Varies by group preference

**5x Multiplier Holes**
- Some groups play 17-18 at 5x the point value of regular holes
- Creates dramatic final moments

---

### E. Scoring & Point Carryover

**Carry-Over / Push Rule**
- When hole results in tie, points don't immediately pay out
- Instead, points "carry over" to next hole, making it worth more
- If next hole also ties, carryover continues to following hole
- Can create huge pots by back nine
- Must be agreed upon BEFORE round begins (optional rule)

**No Carry-Over / Wash**
- Ties result in no points changing hands (standard)
- Hole is a "push" or "wash"

---

### F. Pig Variation (Most Common Secondary Rule)

**Basic Pig Rule:**
- If Wolf picks you as partner, you can throw them back and play solo against all three
- You become the "Pig"
- All bets DOUBLE when Pig is called
- Now it's you alone (Pig) vs. the three others (including your former partner Wolf)
- High risk/high reward for the Pig
- Must happen before leaving tee box

**Pig on Lone Wolf:**
- In some variations, if Wolf goes Lone, one of the three can "counter-pig" and play alone instead
- Creates complex 1v1v2 or 1v3 scenarios (unclear how this resolves)

## 3. Edge Cases & Dispute Scenarios

### Timing Disputes

**Q: Wolf tees FIRST or LAST on each hole?**
- **Tees LAST (more common):** Wolf sees all three tee shots before hitting own shot. Gives information advantage. More strategic.
- **Tees FIRST:** Wolf hits without information. Then watches others. More luck-based, less information advantage.
- **Industry standard:** Most sources (Golf Digest, 18Birdies, Beezer) describe Wolf teeing LAST
- **Real-world finding:** This is heavily debated among groups. Some prefer first, some prefer last. Often tied to what feels fairest to the Wolf.

**Q: Can Wolf change mind about partner after hitting own shot?**
- **Standard answer:** No. Once next player tees off, Wolf's decision is locked in.
- **Flexible groups:** Some allow Wolf to decide after their own shot but before others hit.

**Q: If Wolf hits OB or water, can they still pick a partner?**
- **Standard:** Yes. Bad drive doesn't prevent partnership selection.
- **BUT:** Picking a partner with a bad score makes the partnership unlikely to win, so strategic downside exists.

---

### Partner Selection Disputes

**Q: Can Wolf pick themselves (forced Lone Wolf)?**
- Yes, if they wait until third player hits, they're forced Lone.
- Wolf should make decision before it's forced.

**Q: Timing of "Spit/Divorce" call - when exactly?**
- Sources vary: "before leaving tee box" vs. "before next shot"
- Real world: Groups agree before round.

**Q: Can both teams refuse to accept a Hammer?**
- If one team calls Hammer and other rejects, rejecting team loses hole at old stakes.
- But once Hammer is accepted, accepting team can Re-Hammer.
- Can't hammer twice in a row.

---

### Tie Handling Disputes

**Q: If Wolf's score ties non-Wolf team, what happens?**
- Standard: No points awarded, hole is a wash.
- With carry-over rule: Points push to next hole.
- Some groups: "Best score wins" (so ties stay tied, points split 50/50).

**Q: Does Pig work if both sides tie?**
- Unclear in sources. Likely depends on local rules.

---

### Partner Refusal Scenarios

**Q: If Wolf's picked partner "pigs" them, what's the score?**
- Pig plays alone vs. other three (including Wolf)
- All bets double
- If Pig wins: Pig gets 3 points (or 6 if doubled), others get 0
- If Pig loses: Pig loses 1 point per opponent (or 3 if doubled), others share points

**Q: Can Wolf then pick another partner after being pigged?**
- No. Pig has effectively ended the selection phase. Game becomes 1v3 (Pig vs. the rest).

## 4. Tee Order Debate: First vs. Last

### The Evidence

**"Wolf tees LAST" sources:**
- Golf Digest: "The Wolf goes last so they can see everyone's tee shots before they hit theirs."
- 18Birdies official: Describes Wolf teeing last
- Most instructional content: Wolf as final player in tee order

**"Wolf tees FIRST" sources:**
- Some sources describe Wolf hitting first, then observing
- Golf Crow, various blog posts sometimes use first/last interchangeably

### Real-World Findings

Based on forum discussions and practice, **tee order varies significantly:**
- Traditional tournament rule: Wolf goes LAST (strategic advantage is visibility)
- Some casual groups: Wolf goes FIRST (speeds up play, less "watching and waiting")
- House rule: Groups decide which fits their style

### Stick Implementation Question

**This matters for app:** Does Stick need to support BOTH tee orders, or default to one?

**Recommendation:** Support configurable Wolf tee order. If just one, default to LAST (more common in instructional sources). Allow groups to toggle "Wolf First" if they prefer.

## 5. Player Count Variations

### 3-Player Wolf

**Setup A: Fixed Wolf Position**
- Establish 1-2-3 order with player in position 3 as Wolf on Hole 1
- Rotate each hole (2-3-1 on hole 2, 3-1-2 on hole 3)
- Wolf watches players 1 and 2, picks one as partner
- Creates 2v1 or 1v1v1 (if Wolf goes Lone)

**Setup B: Best Score Determines Wolf**
- Player with second-best score from previous hole is Wolf
- Lowest and highest net scores are the other two

**Partner Picking in 3-Player:**
- Wolf watches player 1 tee
- If Wolf picks player 1: 2v1 (Wolf+Player1 vs. Player2)
- If Wolf doesn't pick player 1, watches player 2
- If Wolf picks player 2: 2v1 (Wolf+Player2 vs. Player1)
- If Wolf picks neither: Forced Lone Wolf (1v2)

**Scoring (3-Player):**
- Wolf wins 2v1: Wolf gets 2, partner gets 2
- Non-Wolf wins vs. Wolf+partner: Non-Wolf gets 3
- Wolf goes Lone and wins: Wolf gets 3 (or 4, varies)
- Wolf goes Lone and loses: Loses 1 point per opponent (or 2, varies)

**KEY FINDING:** 3-player Wolf is less standardized. Groups customize heavily.

**Partner Picking Edge Case - NOT COMMONLY ADDRESSED:**
- Question: In 3-player Wolf, can the Wolf CONDITIONALLY pick both players (like "give me 1+1 instead of 2v1")?
- Answer from research: Not found in standard rules. Appears to not be a feature.
- **For Stick:** This could be a feature gap. Some 3-player groups might want "1+1 vs. 1" (Wolf gets two solo partners playing best ball together, totaling 3 opponents who each play alone).
- **Real world:** Doesn't appear to be standard play. Most 3-player games stick to 2v1 or 1v2.

### 4-Player Wolf (Standard)

- See section 1 above.
- Most research focuses on this format.

### 5-Player Wolf

**Setup:**
- One player rotates through Wolf position each hole
- On Hole 1: Player 5 is Wolf (teeing first or last, debated)
- On Hole 2: Player 1 is Wolf
- On Hole 3: Player 2 is Wolf, etc.

**Partnership:**
- Wolf picks one partner: 2v3 (Wolf + partner vs. three others)
- Wolf goes Lone: 1v4

**Scoring:**
- 2v3 is disadvantageous, so if Wolf's team wins a 2v3 match, points double
- If 2v3 wins: Wolf and partner each get 2 points (doubled)
- If 3v2 wins: Each of the three gets some share (sources vary)
- Lone Wolf risk is even higher (1v4)
- Lone Wolf win: Likely 5-6 points; loss: Likely -3 to -4

**Key finding:** 5-player Wolf is less common but well-documented. Real groups do play this.

## 7. House Rules & Scoring Variations

### Scoring System Variations

**Option A: 1-2-3 System**
- Winning side gets 1 point
- Non-Wolf side beating Wolf+partner gets 2 points
- Not commonly detailed in sources

**Option B: 2-3 System** (Most Common)
- Wolf+partner win: 2 points each
- Non-Wolf team wins: 3 points each
- Lone Wolf win: 4 points
- Lone Wolf loss: -2 points per opponent (or -2 per person, so -6 if 1v3)

**Option C: Point multipliers by hole**
- Holes 1-6: 1x
- Holes 7-12: 2x
- Holes 13-18: 3x
- Or similar escalating system

**Option D: Par-based multipliers**
- Par 3: 1x
- Par 4: 1.5x or 2x
- Par 5: 2x or 3x
- Makes longer holes worth more

**Option E: Handicap adjustment**
- Uses NET scores (Course Handicap applied)
- See Canadian Wolf (section 2.B) or general handicap play

---

### Side Bets & Bonus Points

**Junk Scoring:**
- +1 for natural birdie
- +2 for natural eagle
- -1 for bogey (in some versions)

**Junk with multiplier:**
- Natural birdie = double points for the hole
- Natural eagle = triple points for the hole

**Greenies (Par-3 Bonus):**
- Extra point to player landing closest to pin on par-3
- May be included in hole points or separate bet

**Sandies (Bunker Save):**
- Extra point for holing out from bunker

**Chippies (Chip-In):**
- Extra point for chipping in from off the green

**Closest to Pin (CTP):**
- On designated holes (usually a long par 4 or par 5)
- Winner gets extra point
- Can be separate side bet

**Longest Drive:**
- Winner gets extra point
- Can be separate side bet or included in hole points

**Bingo Bango Bongo combo:**
- Award double points to player winning all three (bingo=first on green, bango=closest to pin, bongo=first to hole out)
- Rarely happens; creates exciting moment when it does

## 8. Final Hole Strategies (Holes 17-18)

### Rules Variations

**Last Place Wolf Rule**
- Trailing player gets Wolf on 17-18
- Gives comeback opportunity
- Source: Multiple sources cite this as standard variation

**Leader Wolf Rule**
- Winning player gets Wolf on 17-18
- Intensifies pressure on leader
- Less commonly documented than Last Place Wolf

**5x or Higher Multiplier**
- 17-18 worth 5x normal hole points
- Creates dramatic finish
- Hole 18 sometimes worth even more (6x or 7x)

**Double Wolf or Extended Wolf**
- Some groups give Wolf 17-18 to whoever isn't Wolf on hole 16
- Ensures continuous Wolf role

---

### Strategic Dynamics on 17-18

**Psychological factors:**
- Trailing player: Aggressive, higher risk tolerance (need points)
- Leading player: Conservative, safety-first (protect lead)
- In-between: Depends on exact gap

**2v2 vs. 1v3 decision:**
- Trailing Wolf: More likely to go Lone on 17-18 (need maximum points)
- Leading Wolf: More likely to pick partner (lock in points, reduce risk)

**Partner selection:**
- Trailing Wolf picks strongest partner available (maximize 2v2 chances)
- Leading Wolf picks weaker partner (split points, reduce risk to themselves)

**Hammer dynamics:**
- Trailing team more likely to call Hammer (need to swing results)
- Leading team more likely to accept or Re-Hammer (confident)

## 11. Newly Identified Variations Not Yet in Stick

### Variation 1: Scotch Wolf (Alternate Shot)

**How it works:**
- Partner selection same as standard Wolf
- After tee shots chosen, teams play alternate shot format
- Better tee shot is kept
- Other player hits second shot
- Alternate from there

**Why it's different:**
- Changes partner evaluation: No longer just "good tee shot" but "good tee shot + good alt-shot player"
- Requires knowledge of teammates' iron/short game skills
- More complex than standard Wolf

**Is this in Stick?**
- Unknown. Check Capabilities Spec for alternate-shot support.

**Feature gap?**
- If Stick supports both best-ball AND alternate-shot modes, Scotch Wolf is possible
- If only best-ball, Scotch Wolf can't be tracked

---

### Variation 2: Canadian Wolf (Net Score Based)

**How it works:**
- Uses Course Handicap (or Playing Handicap) to convert to net scores
- Tee order determined by previous hole's NET score (lowest net tees first, highest becomes Wolf)
- Wolf picks partner based on NET tee shot
- Teams play on NET scores

**Why it's different:**
- Balances skill levels automatically
- Weaker players stay competitive even after bad holes
- Adds handicap complexity

**Is this in Stick?**
- Unknown. Check if Stick has handicap support.

**Feature gap?**
- If Stick tracks only gross scores, Canadian Wolf is not possible
- If Stick integrates WHS or allows manual handicap entry, it could work

---

### Variation 3: Hammer / Re-Hammer

**How it works:**
- Any team can call "Hammer" at any point during hole play (after seeing results, typically)
- Doubles the point value
- Receiving team can accept (and then has option to Re-Hammer) or reject (loses hole at old value)
- Can't hammer twice in a row (teams alternate)

**Why it's different:**
- Dynamic stakes management within a hole
- Adds psychological element and real-time strategy
- No predetermined point values; stakes rise during play

**Is this in Stick?**
- Unknown. Likely not implemented (complex to track).

**Feature gap?**
- Hammer requires:
  - Real-time decision points during play
  - Point multiplier tracking per hole
  - Accept/Reject UI for teams
  - Re-Hammer counter (no double-hammer rule)
  - Probably a post-launch feature

---

### Variation 4: Multi-Multiplier System

**How it works:**
- Different holes worth different amounts
- Example: 1-9 = 1x, 10-16 = 2x, 17-18 = 5x
- Or par-based: Par 3 = 1x, Par 4 = 2x, Par 5 = 3x

**Why it's different:**
- Back-nine intensification common in betting games
- Par-based makes longer holes matter more

**Is this in Stick?**
- Unknown. Check for multiplier support.

**Feature gap?**
- Moderate complexity. Would need:
  - Per-hole multiplier configuration
  - Point calculation: base_points * multiplier

---

### Variation 5: Junk Scoring (Birdies/Eagles as Bonus)

**How it works:**
- Natural birdie adds +1 point (or doubles hole points)
- Natural eagle adds +2 points (or triples hole points)
- Separate from hole winner/loser points

**Why it's different:**
- Rewards good play beyond "winning the hole"
- Creates secondary scoring drama

**Is this in Stick?**
- Unknown. Check if Stick calculates net vs. par.

**Feature gap?**
- Simple if Stick already tracks scores relative to par
- Would need: Birdie detection, Eagle detection, point allocation rules
