# Content Operations Playbook

> **What this is:** A project-agnostic system for running a world-class content operation with an AI partner. Built from real experience — every pattern here was developed, tested, and refined across hundreds of pages of published content.
>
> **How to use it:** Point your AI agent to this document and say: "Build this system for [project name]." The agent should create project-specific versions of every document referenced here, customized to your domain, audience, and goals.
>
> **Origin:** Extracted from the Stick Golf content system (stickapp.golf), March 2026.

## The Three-Document System

Every content operation runs on three living documents. They answer different questions and update on different cadences. Without all three, you're either guessing what to write, guessing when to write it, or guessing whether it's working.

### 1. Content Pipeline — "What to write"

The active queue. Every content opportunity you've identified, organized by status and priority. When a session starts, read this first.

**What it tracks:**

Every piece of content moves through stages:

```
Identified → Researched → Outlined → Drafted → Reviewed → Published → Monitoring
```

**Critical rule:** Always maintain 3-4 pieces at "Researched + Outlined" status. Any session can produce a draft without cold-starting research. This is the difference between a content operation that ships and one that stalls.

**Structure it needs:**

- **Published inventory.** Every live page with its URL and primary keyword. This is your ground truth — what already exists.
- **The queue.** Organized by tier (highest ROI first). Each item includes: status, type, target keyword, strategic rationale, competition assessment, internal linking targets, and outline notes.
- **Guide/pillar expansions.** Quick wins that deepen existing content — new sections, FAQs, examples. These happen alongside new content, not instead of it.
- **The Pounce List.** Pre-loaded triggers for time-sensitive content. External events that create temporary search spikes. You write the angle in advance; when the trigger fires, you draft and publish fast. (See "Pounce List" section below.)
- **Queue management rules.** How to decide what's next when multiple items compete.

**Update cadence:** Every session. After publishing anything, update the status. After research reveals new opportunities, add them.

### 2. Content Calendar — "When to write it"

The timing layer. Content mapped to external moments with publish-by dates.

**What it tracks:**

- **Moment map.** External events your audience cares about, with dates and content opportunities. These are the hooks that make content timely.
- **Publishing rule:** Content tied to a moment publishes **at least 2 weeks before** the moment. Search engines need time to index and rank.
- **Week-by-week schedule** for the current and next month. Specific pieces assigned to specific weeks.
- **Monthly schedule** for the quarter after that. Less granular, more directional.
- **Quarterly cadence** for the rest of the year. Strategic focus per quarter.
- **Content audit schedule.** Monthly, quarterly, and annual review triggers.
- **Publishing checklist.** Every piece passes the same quality gate before going live.

**Update cadence:** Weekly for the near-term schedule. Monthly for the broader plan. After every publish.

### 3. SEO Scoreboard — "What's working"

The feedback loop. Turns you from guessing to data-driven.

**What it tracks:**

- **Site-level metrics** (monthly): total impressions, clicks, CTR, average position, pages indexed.
- **Page-level performance:** top performers (protect them), striking-distance pages (push them to page 1), underperformers (diagnose and fix).
- **Keyword tracking:** primary keywords per page, tracked monthly. Position changes over time.
- **Emerging keywords:** queries you didn't target but are getting impressions for. These reveal new content opportunities.
- **Competitor ranking checks** (quarterly): who ranks where for your primary keywords.
- **Content ROI tracking:** which content drives conversions (downloads, signups, purchases).

**Decision framework built into the scoreboard:**

| Signal | Action |
|--------|--------|
| Page ranking 5-20 with high impressions | **Striking distance.** Expand content, add FAQ, refresh title. Small push could reach page 1. |
| Page ranking 1-5 | **Protect it.** Add internal links from new content. Don't make major changes. |
| Page indexed but 0 impressions after 4 weeks | Check title targeting, cannibalization, internal links. |
| New keyword appearing in emerging list | Check if you have content for it. If not, add to pipeline. If so, optimize. |
| Competitor publishes for your keyword | Don't panic. Check if your content is still better. If not, expand. If yes, monitor 2-4 weeks. |
| CTR below 2% with impressions | Title or meta description isn't compelling. Rewrite. |

**Update cadence:** Monthly once search data is flowing. Weekly for AI citation checks.

## Content Tiering System

Not all content has equal ROI. Organize your pipeline by tiers and work them in order (with exceptions for seasonal timing).

### Tier 1 — Direct Conversion

Content that targets people actively looking for your product category. Highest ROI per piece. These people are closest to becoming users.

*Examples: "Best [product category] apps," "[specific feature] for beginners," player/user-count guides, decision frameworks ("which [thing] should you choose?")*

### Tier 2 — Education & Authority

Content that establishes you as the expert. Targets people who already do the thing your product helps with and want to do it better.

*Examples: Glossaries, etiquette guides, "how to set up [thing] for your group," deep-dive explainers of concepts your audience argues about.*

### Tier 3 — Occasion & Persona

Content that captures people in a specific context or identity. These aren't searching for your product — they're searching for a situation, and your product is the answer.

*Examples: "Best [thing] for [occasion]," "How to run [activity] for [group type]," seasonal content tied to events.*

### Tier 4 — Expansions

Quick wins that deepen existing content. Adding sections, FAQs, examples, or variations to published pages. These happen alongside other tiers, not instead of them.

### Tier 5 — Parked Ideas

Good ideas that aren't worth pursuing yet. Low search volume, too far from conversion, or dependent on something that hasn't happened yet. Review quarterly — some will graduate to higher tiers as the landscape changes.

**The rule:** Tier 1 before Tier 2 before Tier 3. Exceptions only for seasonal timing (a Tier 3 piece tied to an event next week beats a Tier 1 piece with no deadline).

## The Pounce List

Pre-loaded content triggers for external events. The idea: when something happens in your industry, you already know what to write. You're not starting from zero — you're executing a plan.

**Structure:**

For each trigger type, document:
- **What to watch for** (the signal)
- **Content angle** (what to write)
- **Target keyword** (what people will search)
- **Draft timeline** (how fast you need to publish)

**Common trigger types:**

- Industry news / rule changes
- Seasonal events
- Competitor moves (new product, major update, shutdown)
- Viral moments in your space
- New platform features that affect your product
- Cultural moments adjacent to your audience

**How it works in practice:** During every briefing (see below), check if any pounce triggers are active. If one fires, it jumps the queue — draft and publish within 48 hours to capture the search spike.

## The Weekly Intelligence Briefing

At the start of every working session, the AI partner runs a briefing. This is not a static document — it's generated fresh each time based on current data.

**Trigger phrase:** "Run the briefing" (or whatever you prefer — pick one phrase and use it consistently).

### Briefing Checklist

1. **Pipeline check.** Read the content pipeline. What's next? Are there 3-4 pieces at "Researched + Outlined" status? If not, that's job #1.

2. **Calendar check.** Read the content calendar. Is anything due this week or next? Is there an external moment within 3 weeks that requires content? (2-week indexing buffer.)

3. **Scoreboard check.** Read the SEO scoreboard. Has the owner provided new search data? Any striking-distance keywords? Emerging queries?

4. **Trending search scan.** Web search for your primary keywords. Check People Also Ask — new questions you're not covering? Check who ranks and whether their content is better than yours. Check for trending industry news.

5. **Competitor pulse.** Quick check on 2-3 key competitors. Any new content? Site changes? New products?

6. **"What [Owner] Should Know."** Surface insights the owner wouldn't know to ask about. New opportunities, threats, shifts, product insights, time-sensitive opportunities.

7. **Recommended focus.** Based on all of the above: what to work on THIS session (1-2 items), what to prepare for NEXT session, any urgent items.

### Output Format

Conversational summary, not a checklist. Lead with the most important or urgent thing. Be direct. If nothing is urgent, say so and recommend the highest-impact work from the pipeline.

### After the Briefing

If the briefing reveals new insights:
- Add new content opportunities to the pipeline (with source and rationale)
- Update the scoreboard if new ranking data was found
- Log insights in the "What [Owner] Should Know" section of the project context doc

## Writing for AI Search (GEO)

Generative Engine Optimization is the discipline of writing content that AI search engines (Perplexity, ChatGPT, Google AI Overviews) cite. In 2026, this is the primary optimization target. Traditional SEO and GEO want mostly the same thing — but where they diverge, GEO wins.

### Key Data Points

- **53% of AI citations** go to pages under 1,000 words. Length ≠ citations. Stop padding.
- **First-person voice** ("I/we") gets 5.4x more traffic from AI search than third-person.
- **76.4% of ChatGPT citations** come from content updated in the last 30 days. Freshness is massive.
- **FAQ schema gives a 3.2x lift** in AI citation probability.
- **The first 30% of content** gets 44.2% of all citations. Front-load the good stuff.
- **Semantic completeness scores above 0.88** correlate with 7.3x higher citation rates.

### The Dual Optimization Principle

Every section works for two audiences simultaneously:

1. **Humans scanning the page** — they skim headings, read first sentences, decide whether to go deeper.
2. **AI systems extracting passages** — they pull self-contained blocks of 40-80 words that directly answer a question.

What works for one works for the other. Front-load the answer. Make every section standalone. Be specific.

### The First 60 Words Rule

The opening paragraph of every page must:
- Directly answer the core question in plain language
- Include the primary keyword naturally
- Be a complete, self-contained thought that AI can extract verbatim
- Help the reader decide in 5 seconds whether this page is for them

### The Standalone Section Test

Take any H2 section from a page. Read just that section without context. Does it make sense on its own? Can someone understand the answer without the intro? If yes, it's AI-extraction ready. If no, rewrite it.

### Passage Format That Gets Cited

- 40-80 words
- Starts with a direct statement (not a question or throat-clearing)
- Contains at least one specific fact, number, or named example
- Uses the relevant keyword naturally
- Written in first-person where appropriate

### The Freshness Imperative

Monthly content refresh cadence is non-negotiable. Every published page gets a substantive update at least once per month: new FAQ, updated example, new section, rewritten paragraph. Changing the date without changing content doesn't count.

Update `dateModified` in metadata with every substantive edit.

## The Kill List (Anti-AI-Detection)

AI-generated content has tells. Readers notice them even if they can't name them. Your content should be AI-assisted but read like it was written by a practitioner.

### Words and Phrases to Ban

These are the verbal equivalent of a stock photo. Retire them permanently:

| Ban | Use Instead |
|-----|-------------|
| Delve / delve into | Get into, explore, look at |
| It's important to note | (Just say the thing) |
| In today's [anything] | (Delete the whole phrase) |
| Landscape (as metaphor) | Space, world, market |
| Leverage | Use |
| Seamless / seamlessly | Smooth, easy |
| Transformative | (Let the reader decide) |
| Unlock (potential/value) | (Show the value instead) |
| Comprehensive | (Just be comprehensive) |
| Navigate (as metaphor) | Figure out, work through |
| Robust | Reliable, thorough |
| Multifaceted | (Never needed) |
| Revolutionize | (Show, don't tell) |
| It's worth noting | (Just state it) |
| Testament to | (Rephrase entirely) |
| Pivotal | Important, or show why it matters |
| Moreover / Furthermore | And, or new sentence |
| In terms of | (Delete, restructure) |
| Arguably | (Argue or don't) |
| Game-changer | (Show the change) |

### Cheesy Phrases to Ban

Different from robot language — these are "content writer reaching for energy" phrases:

| Ban | Why |
|-----|-----|
| Gets the blood pumping | You're writing a guide, not selling a theme park |
| Where things get interesting | If it's interesting, the reader will notice |
| That's when the real fun begins | Movie trailer voiceover energy |
| Things can get wild | Vague hype — show the specific scenario |
| The drama unfolds | Nobody talks like this |
| The stakes are high | Show stakes with numbers, don't label them |
| You won't want to miss | Infomercial energy |
| At the end of the day | Filler — cut it |
| Here's where it gets good | Trust your content to be good |
| Buckle up | You're writing content, not launching a rocket |

### Structural Patterns That Signal AI

- **Uniform paragraph length.** Mix it up. Some one sentence. Some four.
- **Predictable three-item lists.** Break the pattern. Sometimes two, sometimes five.
- **Restating the intro in the conclusion.** If you've said it, don't say it again.
- **Every sentence starting with "The" or "This."** Vary openers.
- **Hedging.** "It could be argued..." — pick a position. State it directly.
- **Excessive em dashes.** One or two per section max.
- **Numbered headers for everything.** Use numbers when order matters, not by default.

### What Makes Writing Feel Human

- **Sentence length variation.** Short. Then long. That's rhythm.
- **Specificity.** Real names, real numbers, real scenarios.
- **Opinions and asides.** Subjective insights a language model wouldn't volunteer.
- **Imperfection and informality.** Start with "And" or "But." Use fragments for emphasis.
- **References to real culture.** Details that are invisible to AI but immediately recognizable to your audience.

## Voice Framework

Your voice stays the same across all content. Tone adjusts by context. Define both.

### Voice Attributes (Define These Per Project)

Every project needs 4-6 voice attributes. Each one has a "do" and a "don't" — the attribute alone is ambiguous without boundaries.

**Template:**

| Attribute | What It Means | Do | Don't |
|-----------|--------------|-----|-------|
| [e.g., Authoritative] | [definition] | [specific guidance] | [specific anti-pattern] |
| [e.g., Approachable] | [definition] | [specific guidance] | [specific anti-pattern] |
| [e.g., Precise] | [definition] | [specific guidance] | [specific anti-pattern] |
| [e.g., Opinionated] | [definition] | [specific guidance] | [specific anti-pattern] |
| [e.g., Warm] | [definition] | [specific guidance] | [specific anti-pattern] |
| [e.g., Concise] | [definition] | [specific guidance] | [specific anti-pattern] |

### Tone Adjustment by Context

| Context | Tone Shift | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Pillar/guide content | Authoritative teacher | Direct, thorough, shows expertise |
| Strategy content | Knowledgeable friend | Conversational, opinionated |
| Lifestyle content | Relatable insider | Casual, personality-forward |
| Commercial content | Confident pitch | Honest comparison, not sales-y |
| Support/error content | Helpful human | Empathetic, clear |

### Standard Cast (Recurring Characters)

If your content uses examples with named characters, define 3-4 recurring people with different profiles. Readers will recognize them across content. This makes examples feel real rather than abstract.

| Name | Profile | Role in Examples |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| [Name] | [skill level, personality] | [what they represent] |
| [Name] | [skill level, personality] | [what they represent] |
| [Name] | [skill level, personality] | [what they represent] |
| [Name] | [skill level, personality] | [the audience surrogate] |

### Domain-Specific Language Standards

Document the natural language of your audience. Write the way they talk.

- **Always use:** [list of terms and phrasings your audience actually uses]
- **Never use:** [list of overly formal, clinical, or cliché alternatives]
- **The test:** Read the sentence out loud. Would your target user say it naturally? If it sounds like a press release, rewrite it.

## Content Structure Rules

### The Structural Variety Rule

No two pieces of the same type should follow the same section order. This is one of the strongest signals that content was template-generated. Each piece has its own identity — the structure should reflect that.

**Shared across all content:** Voice, specificity, verified math/data, FAQ structure.
**Varies per piece:** Section order, heading style, table formats, CTA length and placement, where examples appear, how strategy is presented.

### Pillar Content Required Elements

These elements appear in every pillar/guide page, but the order, format, and presentation vary:

- **Quick reference data** (can be a box, inline, or woven into the opening)
- **Core explanation with examples** (rules, process, how-to)
- **Advanced/edge case coverage** (what other guides skip)
- **Variations** (alternatives, house rules, regional differences)
- **Worked example with real math** (full scenario, verified numbers)
- **Strategy/tips** (opinionated, backed by reasoning)
- **FAQ section** (5-8 questions, 40-60 word answers, schema markup)
- **CTA** (natural, non-pushy, contextual)

### Blog Post Types

Define 3-4 blog types with different structures and lengths:

| Type | Audience | Structure | Length |
|------|----------|-----------|--------|
| Strategy | Experienced users wanting to improve | Lead with insight → reasoning → exceptions → link to guide | 800-1,200 words |
| How-to | Users with a specific question | Answer immediately → step-by-step → edge cases → guide link | 600-1,000 words |
| Comparison | Users actively choosing a product | Criteria → honest reviews → recommendation → FAQ | 1,500-2,500 words |
| Culture/lifestyle | Users who'll share in their community | Whatever feels right — personality-first | 500-800 words |

## E-E-A-T Implementation

Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness. Both Google and AI systems use this as their primary quality filter.

### Experience

Show you've actually done the thing, not just studied it.

- Reference what you learned building the product
- Use examples from real use (not hypothetical)
- Show edge cases only a practitioner would know

### Expertise

Go deeper than anyone else.

- Show the math. Show the data.
- Cover edge cases competitors skip.
- Explain *why*, not just *what*.
- Use structured data so AI systems can verify depth.

### Authoritativeness

Build over time through breadth and recognition.

- Publish the definitive comparison in your category (an authoritative act)
- Link out to trusted sources (outbound links signal confidence)
- Get mentioned in industry roundups
- Engage authentically in community forums

### Trustworthiness

The foundation everything else builds on.

- Honest competitor comparisons. Don't pretend they don't exist.
- Accurate data. One mistake destroys credibility.
- Transparent about what your product is and costs.
- Corrections policy: update with a note if you get something wrong.

## On-Page SEO Checklist (Every Page)

### Content

- [ ] Opening paragraph answers core question in 40-60 words
- [ ] Each H2 section starts with a standalone answer
- [ ] Target keyword in: title, H1, first 100 words, meta description, URL
- [ ] At least 3 internal links to other pages (contextual, in body)
- [ ] At least 1 outbound link to a trusted external source
- [ ] FAQ section with 5-8 questions, each answered in 40-60 words
- [ ] Real data/math in examples, verified for accuracy
- [ ] No words from the Kill List
- [ ] Sentence length varies naturally
- [ ] At least one opinion or aside showing personality
- [ ] Content is useful without the product pitch
- [ ] No section can be removed without losing value

### Technical

- [ ] Title tag under 60 characters, keyword near front
- [ ] Meta description 140-155 characters with keyword and natural CTA
- [ ] Open Graph tags (title, description, image at 1200x630)
- [ ] Self-referencing canonical URL
- [ ] JSON-LD: Article schema
- [ ] JSON-LD: FAQPage schema (on pillar content)
- [ ] JSON-LD: BreadcrumbList schema
- [ ] All images have descriptive alt text
- [ ] Mobile formatting verified
- [ ] Table of contents with anchor links (pages over 1,000 words)

### Voice

- [ ] Reads like a practitioner explaining, not a content mill
- [ ] Uses first-person voice naturally
- [ ] Uses contractions
- [ ] Uses "you" and "your"
- [ ] Named characters in examples (if applicable)
- [ ] No corporate speak
- [ ] Domain-specific language passes the audience test
- [ ] Maximum one exclamation point per piece
- [ ] No more than two em dashes per section
- [ ] Paragraph length varies
- [ ] Section structure differs from other published pieces (Structural Variety Rule)
- [ ] At least one "we think" / "we found" opinion showing real experience
- [ ] Read-aloud pass completed
- [ ] No cheesy phrases from the ban list
- [ ] Link-back log updated (all existing pages that should link to this new one)

## Session Start Protocol

Add this to your project's context document (CLAUDE.md or equivalent). It tells the AI partner what to do when a new session starts.

```
Every new session should:

1. Read the Content Pipeline — know what's next in the queue
2. Read the Content Calendar — check if anything is time-sensitive
3. Read the SEO Scoreboard — check if new data changes priorities
4. Be ready to draft — there should always be 3-4 pieces at "Researched + Outlined" status

When the owner says "Run the briefing," follow the Weekly Intelligence Briefing protocol.
```

## "What [Owner] Should Know" Section

A running list of insights, observations, and recommendations that surface during research and sessions. Add items here instead of burying them in session history.

Each item includes: the insight, the date, and why it matters.

This section lives in the project context document and grows over time. It's the institutional knowledge that prevents the owner from losing insights between sessions.

## Content Production Workflow

### For Pillar Content (Game Guides, Deep Guides)

1. **Pick the next piece** from the pipeline
2. **Product/feature audit** (if applicable) — verify what the product actually supports before writing about it. Never claim the product does something it doesn't.
3. **Research** — forums, Reddit, community discussions, competitor content. What do users actually do? What questions do they have? What are competitors missing?
4. **Gap analysis** — compare product capabilities vs. real-world usage vs. competitor coverage. Produces three lists: "Product Does This" (highlight), "Common Practice We Don't Support" (document educationally), "Feature Gap" (flag for product team).
5. **Alias/keyword research** — all alternate names, search terms, People Also Ask.
6. **Outline** — map sections, keywords per section, internal link targets.
7. **Draft** — following the content framework. AI-assisted is fine; AI-generated is not.
8. **Read-aloud pass** — every sentence that sounds formal or stumbles gets rewritten.
9. **Owner review** — voice, accuracy, "would I share this?" check.
10. **Technical pass** — schema, metadata, internal links, alt text, mobile.
11. **Publish** — deploy, submit to search engines, share socially.
12. **Link back** — update every existing page that should link to the new piece. Document in link-back log.

### For Blog Posts

Steps 2-4 may be lighter or skipped depending on the post type. Strategy and how-to posts often don't need product audits. Comparison posts need thorough research.

### Monthly Refresh

Every published page gets a substantive update monthly. Not a date change — a real addition: new FAQ, updated example, new section, rewritten paragraph.

### Quarterly Audit

Full review of all content: ranking trends, citation status, math accuracy, broken links, new FAQ questions from actual search queries, internal link opportunities.

## Concept Linking Strategy

Some concepts appear across many pages. Instead of re-explaining them every time (which pads content and patronizes the reader), build dedicated reference pages and link to them.

**How to reference without re-explaining:**

Bad (patronizing): "A handicap is a numerical measure of a golfer's ability, calculated based on their recent scores. A 12-handicap player gets one stroke on each of the 12 hardest holes."

Good (assumes knowledge, links for depth): "Jason at a 12 gets strokes on the 12 hardest holes. ([How handicap strokes work →](/guides/handicaps/))"

The link is there for anyone who needs it. The text doesn't slow down for people who don't. This also creates natural internal links, which helps both SEO and AI citation.

## Bootstrapping This System for a New Project

When setting up for a new project, create these documents in order:

1. **Project context document** (CLAUDE.md) — What is this project? Who's the owner? What exists? What are the goals? Include the Session Start Protocol and "What [Owner] Should Know" section.

2. **Voice & Tone Guide** — Define 4-6 voice attributes with do/don't guidance. Document domain-specific language. Define the standard cast if applicable.

3. **Content Pipeline** — Inventory what's published. Research and identify 15-25 content opportunities. Tier them. Build the Pounce List.

4. **Content Calendar** — Map content to external moments. Build a week-by-week schedule for the next 2 months. Quarterly cadence for 6 months out.

5. **SEO Scoreboard** — Set up tracking tables. Document primary keywords per page. Create the decision framework.

6. **Content Framework** — The master document tying everything together. Kill list, GEO principles, structural rules, checklists, production workflow.

7. **Weekly Briefing Template** — The protocol the AI partner follows when you say "run the briefing."

**Timeline:** Documents 1-5 can be built in a single deep session. Document 6 evolves over the first 3-4 pieces of content. Document 7 is a quick template.

## Queue Management Rules

When multiple pipeline items compete for the next slot:

1. **Tier 1 before Tier 2 before Tier 3.** Higher tiers have higher ROI per piece.
2. **Exception: seasonal timing.** A Tier 3 piece tied to an event next week beats a Tier 1 piece with no deadline.
3. **Exception: pounce triggers.** If an external event fires a pounce trigger, it jumps the queue.
4. **Guide expansions happen alongside other work.** They're quick wins — 30-60 minutes each. Don't let them block new content, but don't ignore them either.
5. **When search data arrives, re-prioritize.** Data beats assumptions. If the scoreboard shows a striking-distance keyword on a Tier 3 page, that page gets priority.
6. **After publishing, update everything.** Pipeline status, calendar dates, link-back log. Don't let documents go stale.

## Competitive Content Monitoring

Check monthly (or during every briefing):

- [ ] Any new blog posts from primary competitors?
- [ ] Any new pages ranking for our target keywords?
- [ ] Any new competitors appearing in our keyword space?
- [ ] Any competitor content that's better than ours? (If yes, improve ours.)
- [ ] Any competitor content gaps we haven't exploited yet?
