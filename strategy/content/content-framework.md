## Part 1: The SEO + AI Search Landscape (2026)

### What Changed

The search world split in two. Traditional Google rankings still matter — but AI-powered search is now the primary discovery channel for a growing share of users. Google AI Overviews appear in roughly 47% of searches. Perplexity has 45+ million monthly users. ChatGPT search has 800+ million weekly active users. Justin gravitates toward AI answers first, and so does our target audience.

The result: organic click-through rates dropped 58-61% when AI Overviews appear. But pages that get *cited* in AI Overviews see 35% more organic clicks than before. Being cited is the new Page 1.

### What This Means for Stick

**AI search is our primary optimization target.** We still respect traditional SEO — but when there's a tension between what Google's blue links want and what AI search engines want, we optimize for AI citation first. The good news: they mostly want the same thing. The best content for AI citation is also the best content for traditional SEO: authoritative, well-structured, specific, and genuinely useful.

The discipline of optimizing for AI search engines has a name: Generative Engine Optimization (GEO). Our framework is GEO-first.

### Key GEO Data Points (2026)

These numbers should inform every content decision we make:

- **53% of AI citations** go to pages under 1,000 words. Length ≠ citations. Stop padding.
- **First-person voice gets cited more** than third-person. Sites using "I/we" saw 5.4x more traffic from AI search than those using "the company."
- **76.4% of ChatGPT citations** come from content updated in the last 30 days. Freshness is massive.
- **FAQ schema gives a 3.2x lift** in AI citation probability. Our FAQ sections are doing heavy lifting.
- **The first 30% of content gets 44.2%** of all AI citations. Front-load the good stuff.
- **Semantic completeness scores above 0.88** correlate with 7.3x higher citation rates. Cover the topic fully, don't just mention it.
- **No Google penalty for AI-assisted content.** Only for low-quality content. Quality is the filter, not origin.

### The Dual Optimization Principle

Every section of every page should work for two audiences simultaneously:

1. **Humans scanning the page** — they skim headings, read the first sentence of each section, and decide whether to go deeper
2. **AI systems extracting passages** — they pull self-contained blocks of 40-80 words that directly answer a question

What works for one works for the other. Front-load the answer. Make every section standalone. Be specific.

## Part 2: Game Guide Structure

Game guides are our pillar content. They need to be the best resource on the internet for each game — not by being the longest, but by being the most useful.

### The Structural Variety Rule (CRITICAL)

**No two game guides should follow the same section order.** This is one of the strongest signals that content was generated from a template — and both human readers and AI systems can detect it. Each game has its own identity, and the guide structure should reflect that.

Nassau leads with the three-bet structure because that's what defines it. Wolf leads with the rotation mechanic because partner selection is the whole game. Skins leads with the one-rule simplicity and carryover drama because that's the hook.

What this means in practice:

- Vary where history/origin appears (some guides weave it into the rules, some mention it in passing, some skip it entirely)
- Vary how strategy and mistakes are presented (paragraphs in one, conversational blend in another, bold-then-explain only when it genuinely fits)
- Vary the CTA length and placement
- Vary table formats and when tables appear
- Move sections around based on what matters most for that specific game

Every guide should feel like it was written by the same person explaining a different game — not the same template filled with different content. The shared elements are voice, specificity, settlement math, and FAQ structure. Everything else can flex.

### The First 60 Words Rule

The opening paragraph of every game guide is the most important text on the page. It needs to:

- Directly answer "What is [Game]?" in plain language
- Include the primary keyword naturally
- Be a complete, self-contained thought that AI can extract verbatim
- Help the reader decide in five seconds whether this game is for them

This isn't just an SEO tactic. It respects the reader's time. Someone searching "nassau golf" might want a quick refresher before their Saturday round. Give them the answer immediately, then offer depth for those who want it.

**Example (Nassau):**

> Nassau is golf's most popular betting format — three separate matches rolled into one round. You're betting on the front nine, back nine, and overall 18 holes independently, which means you can lose the front, win the back, and still break even. Add presses and it gets interesting fast.

That's 50 words. It answers the question, explains why the game is interesting, and hints at depth to come. A reader knows immediately whether to keep going. An AI system can extract it as a complete answer.

**What to avoid:**

> Welcome to our comprehensive guide on Nassau golf! In this article, we'll delve into everything you need to know about this beloved betting format. Whether you're a seasoned golfer or just getting started with golf betting, this guide will help you understand the ins and outs of Nassau.

That's 50 words of nothing. No information. No answer. No reason to keep reading. And it reads like it was generated by a machine.

### Game Guide Required Elements

Every game guide must include these elements, but the **order, format, and presentation should vary** per the Structural Variety Rule. These are ingredients, not a recipe.

#### Quick Reference Data

Player count, scoring type, common stakes, alternate names, Stick features. Can be a code block, can be inline after the intro, can be woven into the opening paragraph. Don't always use the same format.

#### Rules with Examples

Core rules explained with examples using our standard cast (Justin, Jason, Evan, Todd). Every rule should be followed by what it looks like in practice. Don't just say "the match is played in match play format." Show it: "Justin wins the 3rd hole with a net birdie. He goes 1-up on the front nine match."

Structure subheadings around how people actually search: "How Scoring Works" (targets "nassau scoring"), "What Are Presses?" (targets "nassau press"). Each subheading should begin with a standalone 40-60 word answer before expanding.

#### Settlement Math

This is where we destroy the competition. Beezer's game pages have no settlement examples. Most golf sites say "the loser pays the winner" and leave it at that. We show the actual math. Full scenario. Named players. Every dollar tracked. Zero-sum verified.

This is also our strongest E-E-A-T signal. We can show settlement math because we built calculators that handle it.

#### Variations

Each variation gets a full explanation — not a bullet point. Explain how it changes the game and when it's a good idea. Weave opinions in: "We think the weighted overall makes for a better game." These can live in their own section or be threaded throughout the guide where they're contextually relevant.

#### Strategy and Mistakes

Specific, opinionated, backed by reasoning. Not generic advice.

Bad: "Consider pressing when you're behind."
Good: "Pressing 2-down with 5 holes left is almost always correct. You're probably losing that bet anyway — the press gives you a chance to recover half."

**Format these differently across guides.** Nassau uses flowing paragraphs under "What New Players Get Wrong." Wolf uses bold-then-explain under separate Strategy and Mistakes sections. Skins blends both into a single conversational "Getting the Most Out of Skins" section. Vary the approach.

#### FAQ Section (5-8 Questions)

Source questions from Google's "People Also Ask," golf forums, Reddit. This is our highest-value section for AI citation (3.2x lift with FAQ schema).

Rules for FAQ answers: answer in 2-3 sentences (40-60 words), each answer self-contained (no referencing other parts of the guide), include the question keyword naturally, use FAQPage JSON-LD schema.

#### CTA

Short, natural, at the end. Not a sales pitch. "Stick tracks every bet — the original three, every press, every press-the-press — with a line-item settlement." One secondary lighter mention can appear mid-content where contextually relevant. Vary the length and tone across guides.

### Game Guide Length

**53% of AI citations go to pages under 1,000 words.** Length does not correlate with citations or rankings. Stop thinking in word counts.

A complex game like Wolf with Pig, Hammer, Blind Wolf, carryover, and multiplier stacking genuinely needs 2,000+ words. A simpler game like Snake might be 800-1,200 and that's perfect. The guide should read like someone sitting down to explain everything you need to know — if they'd naturally stop at 900 words, we stop at 900 words.

The tests: Would removing any section make the guide less useful? Does every paragraph earn its spot? Would you actually read this whole thing before your Saturday round, or would you skim past the filler? If there's filler, cut it.

## Part 3: Blog Post Structures

Blog posts serve different purposes and need different structures. One template for all posts would feel formulaic and read like a content mill.

### Strategy Posts (800-1,200 words)

These extend game guides with specific, actionable advice.

**Structure:**

1. **Lead with the insight** (first 60 words) — state the conclusion upfront. "Pressing 2-down on 15 is almost always worth it, and here's the math to prove it."
2. **The reasoning** — explain why, with scenarios and numbers
3. **The exceptions** — when the advice doesn't apply
4. **Connection to game guide** — natural link back to the parent game guide

**Opening example:**

> Here's something most Nassau players get wrong: they treat pressing as an emotional decision. They press when they're frustrated, not when the math makes sense. But pressing has a breakeven point, and once you understand it, you'll know exactly when a press is smart and when it's just throwing money away.

### How-To Posts (600-1,000 words)

These answer a specific question. Utility content — get to the answer fast.

**Structure:**

1. **Answer the question** (first 40 words) — directly, completely, no preamble
2. **Step-by-step detail** — expand with examples
3. **Edge cases** — what if something unusual happens
4. **Link to full game guide** — for readers who want deeper context

**Opening example (how to settle a nassau):**

> A Nassau settles as three separate bets. Count wins on the front nine, back nine, and overall 18 — each is its own match. The loser of each match pays the agreed wager. If you played presses, each press settles independently too. Here's a full walkthrough with real numbers.

### Comparison Posts (1,500-2,500 words)

These target golfers actively choosing an app. High conversion intent.

**Structure:**

1. **State the comparison clearly** — what we're comparing and why
2. **Evaluation criteria** — what matters (game selection, scoring accuracy, ease of use, price)
3. **Each option reviewed honestly** — name competitors, describe strengths and weaknesses
4. **Recommendation** — where Stick fits, without overselling
5. **FAQ** — common questions about choosing a golf betting app

**The honesty rule:** Never trash competitors. Name what they do well. Explain where Stick differs. Readers trust honest comparisons — and dishonest ones get called out in Reddit threads.

### Culture/Lifestyle Posts (500-800 words)

These build brand personality and are designed to be shared in group chats. SEO is secondary — shareability is primary.

**Structure:** Whatever feels right. Storytelling, observations, humor. These should read like they were written by someone in the Saturday morning group, not a content team.

**The litmus test:** Would someone screenshot this and send it to their golf group chat? If not, it's not interesting enough.

## Part 4: Writing Voice — How Stick Sounds

### The Core Voice

Every piece of Stick content should sound like Justin sitting down to brain-dump what he knows about a game. Not polished content marketing. Not a textbook. Just someone who built a golf betting app because he plays these games every week and got tired of the group chat arguments about who owes what.

First-person voice ("we," "our rounds," "in our experience") is not just a style choice — it's a GEO advantage. Sites using first-person voice see 5.4x more traffic from AI search than those using third-person. AI systems cite authentic perspectives over institutional-sounding content.

### Voice Attributes (Refined)

**Authentic, not polished.** We're not a content team. We're a designer who built a golf app and the AI that helps him write about it. The writing should reflect that — real opinions, real experiences, real stakes. "We play auto-press in most of our rounds because it keeps the pace moving" is better than "Auto-press is a popular option among experienced players."

**Knowledgeable, not academic.** We know these games inside out because we built software that calculates every edge case. But we explain them like we're talking at the turn, not writing a textbook.

**Specific, not vague.** Real names (Justin, Jason, Evan, Todd). Real numbers ($5-$5-$10, not "a small wager"). Real holes (the 14th, not "a par 3"). Specificity is what separates expert writing from generic content.

**Opinionated, not neutral.** We have views and we share them. "Wolf is the most strategic game in golf." "We think the weighted overall makes for a better game, but plenty of groups prefer equal amounts." Opinions backed by reasoning signal expertise and create content worth sharing. AI systems cite opinionated expert content more than neutral summaries.

**Warm, not sentimental.** We love golf and the culture around it — the bets, the trash talk, the group chat chaos. But we don't get saccharine about it. No "golf is a beautiful journey." We're practical people who like to play and compete.

**Concise, not padded.** Say it and move on. If a section is done, it's done. Don't add a summary paragraph restating what was just said. If the game is simple, the guide should be short. Nobody respects content that wastes their time to hit a word count.

### Who We're Writing For

Our reader plays golf regularly — weekly or close to it — and has been around the game long enough to know the basics. They know what a birdie is. They know what handicaps are in general terms. They've probably played a Nassau or skins at some point, even if they didn't know the exact rules or got the settlement wrong afterward. They found us because they searched a specific game rule, wanted to settle an argument with their group, or were looking for a new game to suggest for Saturday.

They don't need us to explain what a par is. They also don't need us to assume they've played every variation of every game — plenty of golfers have played Nassau for years without understanding presses, or played skins without knowing about carryover options.

The calibration: write for the golfer who's been playing money games casually and wants to play them correctly. They'll skim past anything that feels like a beginner tutorial, but they'll appreciate a clear explanation of how settlement math actually works because they've been doing it wrong in the parking lot.

**What this means practically:**

- Never explain birdie, bogey, par, handicap index, or stroke index inline. These are assumed knowledge.
- Do explain game-specific mechanics (presses, carryovers, Pig, Blind Wolf) because even experienced golfers play these differently.
- When we reference handicap strokes being applied to specific holes, we don't need to explain what a handicap is — we just need to show how it affects scoring in this game.
- Use golf language naturally. "Makes birdie" not "scores one under par." "Three-putts" not "takes three putts on the green." If you have to explain it, you're writing for the wrong audience.

### Golf Language Standards

Pulled from how golfers actually talk. Every piece of content should pass the "would someone in your foursome say this?" test.

**Always use:**

- "makes birdie" / "made eagle" (not "achieved birdie" or "scored a birdie")
- "three-putts" / "three-jacked" (not "had a three-putt")
- "gets a stroke" / "gets a shot" (not "receives a handicap stroke")
- "presses" (not "initiates a press bet")
- "All Square" (not "tied" in match play context)
- "dormie" (one side leads by the number of holes remaining — they can't lose in regulation)
- "closed out" / "clinched" (match won before 18)
- "halved" (tied hole in match play, not "draw" or "tie")
- "net score" (after handicap strokes applied)
- "the number" (a player's expected score on a hole given strokes)
- "the turn" (the transition between front and back nine)

**Never use:**

- "achieved" anything — too formal for golf
- "executed" — too clinical, nobody "executes" a press
- "dominated" — too aggressive for golf culture
- "crushing it" — too slangy for our tone
- Financial metaphors ("climbs out of the red," "in the black") — we're not a trading desk
- Combat metaphors ("draws first blood," "destroyed") — golf isn't a fight
- Generic sports clichés ("brought their A-game," "stepped up big") — every content mill uses these

**The test:** Read the sentence out loud. Would you say it standing on the 10th tee waiting for the group ahead to clear? If it sounds like a press release or a video game, rewrite it.

### Golf Shorthand Rule

When describing scoring scenarios, use parenthetical notation the way golfers do in conversation. Less is more when the reader already speaks the language.

Bad: "Jason makes bogey on a stroke hole, that nets to par. If Justin pars, the hole is halved."
Good: "Jason bogies with a stroke (net par). Justin pars. Hole halved."

Bad: "if Jason's gross score is one higher than Justin but he's getting a stroke, they tie the hole."
Good: "Jason's gross 5 becomes a net 4 with the stroke."

The pattern: state what happened, parenthetical for the net result, move on. Don't narrate the arithmetic — golfers can do the math. This keeps scoring scenarios tight and natural instead of reading like a tutorial.

### Concept Linking Strategy (Don't Re-Explain Everything)

Some concepts come up in every game — handicaps, stroke index, net scoring, match play format. Instead of re-explaining these in every guide (which pads the content and patronizes the reader), we build dedicated reference pages and link to them.

**Planned reference pages:**

- **How Handicaps Work in Golf Betting** — one deep guide covering handicap application, stroke index, net vs. gross, when to use each. Every game guide links here instead of explaining handicaps inline.
- **How Settlement Works** — the concept of pairwise settlement, zero-sum verification, pot models. Game guides show the specific math but link here for the underlying logic.
- **What Are Presses in Golf Betting** — presses appear in Nassau and several other games. One thorough explanation, linked from everywhere it's relevant.

**How to reference without re-explaining:**

Bad (patronizing): "Handicap strokes are distributed across the 18 holes based on the course's stroke index, which ranks holes by difficulty. A 12-handicap gets one stroke on each of the 12 hardest holes."

Good (assumes knowledge, links for depth): "Jason at a 12 gets strokes on the 12 hardest holes. On those holes, his gross 5 becomes a net 4. ([How handicap strokes work in golf betting →](/guides/handicaps/))"

The link is there for anyone who needs it. The text doesn't slow down for people who don't. This also creates more internal links naturally, which helps both traditional SEO and AI citation (more interconnected pages signal topic authority).

## Part 5: Writing Like a Human (Anti-AI-Detection Guide)

AI-generated content has specific tells. Readers notice them, even if they can't name them. And while Google doesn't penalize AI content directly, they penalize low-quality content — and AI tells are a quality signal to readers.

Our content will be AI-assisted (Claude helps draft and structure) but needs to read like it was written by a person who plays golf and built a betting app. Here's how.

### Words and Phrases That Scream AI

Never use these in any Stick content. They're the verbal equivalent of a stock photo.

**The Kill List:**

| Word/Phrase | Why It's a Problem |
|---|---|
| Delve / delve into | The single most-flagged AI word. Retire it permanently. |
| It's important to note | Filler. If it's important, just say it. |
| In today's [anything] | "In today's fast-paced world..." is the AI equivalent of "Dear Sir/Madam." |
| Landscape (as metaphor) | "The golf betting landscape" — nobody talks like this. |
| Leverage | Use "use." |
| Seamless / seamlessly | Means nothing. Show what's smooth instead of claiming it. |
| Transformative | Let the reader decide if something is transformative. |
| Unlock (potential/value) | Marketing speak. |
| Comprehensive | Don't tell people the guide is comprehensive. Just make it comprehensive. |
| Navigate (as metaphor) | "Navigate the world of golf betting" — who writes like this? |
| Cutting-edge | Show, don't tell. |
| Robust | Say "reliable" or "thorough." |
| Multifaceted | Never needed. |
| Revolutionize | The most oversold word on the internet. |
| It's worth noting | Same as "it's important to note." Delete and just state the thing. |
| Testament to | Pompous. |
| Pivotal | Use "important" or better yet, show why something matters. |
| Game-changer | Ironic given our industry, but still banned. |
| Embark on a journey | We're explaining how to bet on golf, not writing a fantasy novel. |
| Moreover / Furthermore | Over-formal connectors. Use "and" or start a new sentence. |
| In terms of | Delete it. Restructure the sentence. |
| Arguably | Either argue the point or don't. |

### Cheesy Phrases (The Other Kill List)

The AI Kill List catches robot language. This list catches content-writer language — phrases a human wouldn't say but a writer reaching for energy would. These are different problems with the same effect: the reader trusts you less.

| Phrase | Why It's Cheesy |
|---|---|
| "gets the blood pumping" | You're selling a game guide, not a theme park ride. |
| "where things get interesting" | If it's interesting, the reader will notice. You don't need to announce it. |
| "that's when the real fun begins" | Same energy as a movie trailer voiceover. |
| "things can get wild" | Vague hype. Show the specific scenario that's wild. |
| "the drama unfolds" | Nobody at your club talks like this. |
| "it's a whole different ballgame" | Cliché. Just describe how it's different. |
| "the stakes are high" | Show the stakes with numbers. Don't label them. |
| "you won't want to miss" | Infomercial energy. |
| "at the end of the day" | Filler phrase. Cut it and start with whatever comes next. |
| "it all comes down to" | Almost always followed by something obvious. |
| "here's where it gets good" | Trust your content to be good without warning the reader. |
| "buckle up" | We're explaining golf bets, not launching a rocket. |

**The filter:** If the phrase could appear in a YouTube thumbnail or a clickbait headline, it doesn't belong in our content. Let the math and the scenarios create the tension. A 5-skin carryover is inherently dramatic — you don't need to tell the reader it's dramatic.

### Structural Patterns That Signal AI

**Uniform paragraph length.** AI writes paragraphs of nearly identical length. Mix it up. Some paragraphs should be one sentence. Others can be four. Variation creates rhythm.

**Predictable three-item lists.** AI loves grouping things in threes: "clear, effective, and reliable." Break the pattern. Sometimes list two things. Sometimes five. Sometimes don't list at all.

**Restating the introduction in the conclusion.** AI conclusions repeat 70-80% of the intro. If you've said it, don't say it again. A conclusion should add something — a final thought, a recommendation, a call to action — not a summary.

**Every sentence starting with "The" or "This."** Read back through a draft. If three consecutive sentences start with the same word, rewrite.

**Hedging.** "It could be argued that..." "Many golfers find that..." "It's generally considered..." Pick a position. State it directly. This is one of the biggest separators between AI-assisted writing and AI-generated writing.

**Excessive em dashes.** One or two per piece is fine — they're a real punctuation mark that real writers use. But AI overuses them. If you've got more than two in a section, replace some with commas, periods, or parentheses.

**Numbered headers for everything.** AI defaults to "1. First Section, 2. Second Section, 3. Third Section." Use numbered lists when order matters (setup steps, scoring sequence). Use unstructured headings when it doesn't.

### What Makes Writing Feel Human

**Sentence length variation.** This matters more than anything else. Mix short punchy statements with longer, more complex ones. "Justin wins the front nine. That puts him $5 up before the back nine even starts — and with a press already running from the 7th hole, the math is about to get interesting." Short. Then long. That's rhythm.

**Specificity.** Generic: "Players can add extra bets during the round." Human: "Jason presses on the 12th tee, two down with seven holes left. That starts a new $5 match from 12 through 18."

**Opinions and asides.** "Wolf is probably the most strategic game in golf — which is exactly why some groups avoid it." That parenthetical opinion is something a human would say. AI wouldn't volunteer a subjective aside.

**Imperfection and informality.** Start a sentence with "And" or "But." Use a fragment for emphasis. "Every dollar." End a paragraph on an incomplete thought that the next paragraph picks up. These small irregularities are what make writing feel alive.

**References to real golf culture.** The group chat. The argument about whether someone pressed or not. The guy who wants to play Wolf every week. The Saturday morning tee time text thread. These details are invisible to AI but immediately recognizable to golfers.

## Part 6: E-E-A-T Implementation

E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) is Google's content quality framework. In 2026, it's also the primary filter AI systems use to decide which sources to cite. For a new domain like ours, E-E-A-T signals need to be deliberate.

### Experience

We've built the calculators. We've tested 1,448+ scenarios. We've played these games ourselves. This isn't theoretical knowledge — it's practitioner knowledge.

**How to signal it:**

- Reference what we learned building Stick: "We built Stick's Nassau engine to handle press chains automatically — and in testing, we found that most groups get the settlement math wrong on at least one press per round."
- Use examples that show we've played: "In our experience, four-player Wolf works best when the lone wolf option is mandatory at least once every four holes."
- Show edge cases only a practitioner would know: "What most guides skip: what happens when all four players tie a hole in nine-point? The answer depends on your group's house rules, and we've seen three different approaches."

### Expertise

Our content goes deeper than anything else available. Settlement math, edge cases, variation documentation, strategy reasoning.

**How to signal it:**

- Show the math, always. Every dollar, every calculation.
- Cover edge cases that competitors skip.
- Explain the *why* behind rules, not just the *what*.
- Use structured data (Article, FAQPage schema) so AI systems can verify our depth.

### Authoritativeness

This builds over time through backlinks, citations, and recognition. We can accelerate it.

**How to signal it:**

- Publish the "Best Golf Betting Apps" comparison (an authoritative act — you have to know the space to compare it fairly).
- Link out to trusted sources (USGA, golf publications) — outbound links to authoritative domains signal confidence.
- Get listed in golf app roundups (outreach to Golf Monthly, MyGolfSpy, Today's Golfer).
- Engage genuinely on Reddit and GolfWRX when betting game questions come up.

### Trustworthiness

Trust is the foundation. Everything else builds on it.

**How to signal it:**

- Honest competitor comparisons. Don't pretend Beezer and Skins App don't exist.
- Accurate math. If we get a settlement wrong, we lose credibility instantly.
- Transparent about what Stick is and costs. No hidden fees, no tricks.
- Corrections policy: if we get something wrong, update the page with a note.

## Part 7: GEO (Generative Engine Optimization)

GEO is our primary optimization discipline. Traditional SEO matters, but AI search citation is where the growth is. This section governs how we write for AI extraction.

### Technical Prerequisites

Our `robots.txt` must allow all major AI crawlers:

```
User-agent: GPTBot
Allow: /

User-agent: PerplexityBot
Allow: /

User-agent: ClaudeBot
Allow: /

User-agent: GoogleBot
Allow: /

User-agent: *
Allow: /
Disallow: /api/
Disallow: /_next/
```

If any of these are blocked, we're invisible to that platform.

### Content Structure for AI Extraction

AI systems break pages into passages and evaluate each one independently. Every section needs to function as a standalone answer. The first 30% of any page gets 44.2% of all citations — front-load the important stuff.

**The standalone test:** Take any H2 section from a game guide. Read just that section, without any context from the rest of the page. Does it make sense on its own? Can someone understand the answer without having read the intro? If yes, it's AI-extraction ready.

**Passage format that gets cited:**

- 40-80 words
- Starts with a direct statement (not a question or throat-clearing)
- Contains at least one specific fact, number, or named example
- Uses the relevant keyword naturally
- Written in first-person ("we," "our") where appropriate — 5.4x citation advantage

### What Gets Cited (Priority Order)

1. **Original data and statistics.** "In Stick's testing, the average Nassau round generates 2.3 presses" is more citable than "many rounds include presses."
2. **Expert explanations with specifics.** Named examples, actual math, real scenarios.
3. **Structured Q&A pairs.** FAQ sections with FAQPage schema give a 3.2x citation lift. This is our highest-leverage element.
4. **First-person opinions with reasoning.** "We play auto-press because it keeps the pace moving" gets cited more than "auto-press is commonly used."
5. **Definitions and concept explanations.** "What is a Nassau?" answered in 40-60 words.
6. **Step-by-step procedures.** Setup instructions, scoring procedures, settlement walkthroughs.

### Semantic Completeness

Pages with semantic completeness scores above 0.88 see 7.3x higher citation rates. This means covering the full topic, not just the basics. For a Nassau guide, that means presses, auto-press, handicaps, settlement math, team play, variations, and common mistakes — not just "here are the three bets."

This is where our depth is a genuine advantage. Beezer's 300-word game pages can't achieve semantic completeness. Our 1,500-2,500 word guides can — and the data says AI systems reward that comprehensiveness heavily.

### The Freshness Imperative

**76.4% of ChatGPT citations come from content updated in the last 30 days.** This is the single most actionable data point in GEO.

What it means for us:

- **Monthly content refresh cadence.** Every published game guide gets a meaningful update at least once per month. Not a date change — a real addition. New FAQ question, updated settlement example, new variation documented, strategy tip added.
- **dateModified matters.** Update the dateModified frontmatter every time a page gets a substantive edit. AI crawlers check this.
- **New content gets cited fast.** Perplexity crawls in real-time. A new game guide can get cited within hours of publishing. We should publish and then immediately check Perplexity for our target queries.

## Part 9: Alternate Names & Alias Strategy

Golf betting games go by different names depending on region, friend group, and how the game got passed down. Your buddy calls it "6 point" — it's Scotch. Someone else plays "Dots" — that's Junk. This is extremely common, and it's an SEO goldmine.

### Why This Matters

Someone searching "dots golf game" is looking for Junk. If our Junk guide doesn't mention Dots, Trash, and Garbage, that person never finds us. Worse, they find a competitor. Alternate names are free keyword targets with almost zero competition — because nobody else is systematically capturing them.

### The Alias Research Step

Before writing any game guide, research all known alternate names for that game. This is now a required step in the production workflow (Step 2). Sources to check:

- Reddit r/golf threads ("what's the golf game called where...")
- GolfWRX forums
- Golf Digest, Golf Monthly game explainers
- Beezer's game pages (they sometimes mention alternates)
- Google's "People Also Ask" for the game name
- Direct searches for suspected aliases

### How to Use Aliases in Content

**In the opening paragraph:** Mention the most common alternates naturally. "Junk — sometimes called Dots, Trash, or Garbage depending on your group — tracks side bets for achievements like greenies, sandies, and barkies throughout the round."

**In an "Also Known As" line** in the Quick Reference Box:

```
Also called: Dots, Trash, Garbage
```

**In the FAQ section:** Include at least one alias question. "Is Dots the same as Junk?" gets its own FAQ entry with schema markup. That's a direct keyword capture.

**In the meta description:** If an alternate name has significant search volume, work it in. "Junk (also called Dots or Trash) is a golf side bet game..."

**Do NOT create separate pages** for each alternate name. That's duplicate content. One comprehensive page per game, with aliases woven throughout, is the right approach. The aliases bring people to the same authoritative page.

### Master Alias Reference

See `strategy/content/game-aliases.md` for the complete alias map. Quick reference for the highest-value aliases:

| Game | High-Value Aliases | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Junk | Dots, Trash, Garbage | Heavily searched under all four names |
| Scotch | Six-Point, 6-Point Scotch | Many groups call it "6 point" without knowing it's Scotch |
| Sixes | Hollywood, Round Robin | Three roughly equal search terms |
| Wolf | Ship, Captain & Crew | Regional, less common but real searches |
| Nine Point | Nines, 5-3-1 | "Nines" may be more common than "Nine Point" |
| Split Sixes | English, Cricket | "English" is common in certain regions |
| Quota | Chicago, Point Quota | "Chicago" is equally valid and widely used |
| Snake | Animals, Zoo Golf | The broader "animal game" category |
| Vegas | Monte Carlo (variation) | Monte Carlo is technically a variant, but people search for it |

### The Ecosystem Update (Step 12 — Think Holistically)

Step 12 is more than just adding links. Every new piece of content exists within an ecosystem of 20+ published pages, and research done for the new piece often reveals insights that improve existing pages too. The agent must think holistically — not just "what does the new article need?" but "what does the new article's research tell us about the rest of the site?"

**There are three layers to this step, in order:**

#### Layer 1: Content Updates to Existing Pages

During research (Part 11), the agent will learn things that are relevant to pages we've already published. These aren't just linking opportunities — they're content improvements.

**Ask:** "Did my research reveal anything that makes an existing page more useful, more timely, or more complete?"

Examples of what this looks like in practice:
- Writing a TGL Hammer article → research shows TGL has made hammer mainstream → the Wolf guide's hammer section should acknowledge this ("If you've watched TGL, you've seen the hammer — it's the same mechanic"). One sentence. Huge relevance boost.
- Writing a 3-player games post → research surfaces a Wolf 3-player variation you didn't know about → the Wolf guide should add it to its variations section.
- Writing a beginners guide → research reveals golfers confuse "press" and "hammer" → the Presses reference guide could add a one-line clarifier: "A press is different from a hammer — see [Hammer vs Press](/blog/hammer-vs-press/) for the distinction."
- Research surfaces a trending YouTube channel (Good Good, Bob Does Sports) playing a game Stick supports → existing game guide should mention it as a cultural reference point.

**The rule:** If research for the new piece reveals something that would make an existing page better, update the existing page too. Don't hoard the insight for just the new article.

**Document every ecosystem update:**
```
## Ecosystem Updates from [New Article Title] — [Date]
| Page Updated | What Changed | Why |
|---|---|---|
| /games/wolf/ | Added TGL reference to hammer section | TGL popularized hammer; acknowledging it makes the guide more current |
| /blog/nassau-vs-skins-vs-wolf/ | Added note about TGL hammer in Wolf section | Cultural moment connection |
| /guides/presses/ | Added one-line hammer distinction | Prevents confusion surfaced in research |
```

1. **Check the Internal Link Relatedness Map** (Appendix B) for which pages should link to the new one.
2. **Open each related page** and find a natural place to add a contextual link. Not a "see also" footer — a link woven into a sentence where the new page is genuinely relevant. If you made content updates in Layer 1, the links often fit naturally into those updates.
3. **Document what you changed** in a link-back log entry:

```
## [New Page Title] — Published [Date]
| Updated Page | Anchor Text | Location in Page |
|---|---|---|
| /games/wolf/ | "TGL brought hammer to a national audience" | Hammer section |

| /blog/best-golf-betting-apps-2026/ | (no natural link found) | — |
```

4. **Check for reverse opportunities** — does the new page link back to existing pages? Every page should link to at least 5 other pages. If the new piece only links to 2-3, find places to add more.

#### Layer 3: Freshness Timestamps

Every existing page that received a substantive content update (Layer 1) or meaningful new context (not just a link addition) should get its `updatedAt` frontmatter bumped to today's date. This signals freshness to AI crawlers (76.4% of ChatGPT citations come from content updated in the last 30 days). A single link addition doesn't warrant a date bump, but adding a TGL reference to the Wolf guide does.

This creates a deliberate, traceable ecosystem rather than a collection of isolated pages. The log lives at `content/link-back-log.md` and gets checked during quarterly audits.

### The Audit-First Rule

**Never write a game guide without first auditing what the app actually does.**

This is non-negotiable. The audit serves two purposes:

**For the content:** We only claim Stick handles features that are actually implemented. If the Nassau engine doesn't support Aloha bets, we can still explain Aloha as a variation golfers play — but we don't say "Stick tracks your Aloha." This keeps our credibility intact.

**For the app:** The research that informs the content is also a quality check on the app itself. If research reveals that most groups play with a "no press after hole 7" restriction and the app doesn't support configurable press cutoffs, that's a feature gap worth fixing. The content workflow feeds improvements back to the product.

**How to run an audit:**

Ask Claude Code (in the app repo) for a complete feature audit of the target game engine. The prompt should request:

- Every supported feature and configuration option
- Default values for all settings
- Edge cases the engine handles
- What the engine does NOT support
- Any known limitations or planned additions

Bring the audit output back to this workspace. The content writer (Claude in this repo) compares the audit against game rules research and produces:

1. **A "Stick Does This" list** — features to highlight in the guide
2. **A "Common Variation" list** — things golfers do that Stick doesn't track (document educationally, no false product claims)
3. **A "Feature Gap" list** — things the app should probably support (tasks for Claude Code)

This three-list output should be documented per game and stored alongside the game guide draft for reference.

### Monthly Content Refresh (GEO Critical)

Content freshness is a major signal for AI citation. This makes regular refreshes non-negotiable — but "regular" doesn't mean "every page every month." As our content library grows, we need a tiered model to stay sustainable.

**Tiered Refresh Model:**

| Tier | Which pages | Refresh cadence | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1** | Pages with Search Console impressions, commercial intent pages, pages cited by AI | Every 30 days | These are working. Keep them fresh to maintain and grow citations. |
| **Tier 2** | Mid-tail game guides, supporting blog posts | Every 60-90 days | Important but not urgent. Refresh when new research or a new content piece creates a natural reason to update. |
| **Tier 3** | Long-tail niche pages with minimal impressions | Every 6 months | Don't waste effort on pages that aren't getting traction. If a Tier 3 page starts getting impressions, promote it to Tier 2. |

**Right now (20 pages):** Everything is effectively Tier 1 or 2. The monthly-content-refresh scheduled task picks the top 5 pages to refresh each month — that's sustainable and focuses effort where it matters.

**At 40+ pages:** The tiered model prevents an operational bottleneck. You can't substantively update 40 pages monthly — but you can update 8-10 high-performing ones and let the rest cycle on a longer cadence.

**What counts as a "substantive update":** New FAQ question added, settlement example updated or added, new variation documented, strategy tip added based on real play experience, section rewritten for clarity, new internal link to recently published content, cultural reference added (e.g., TGL mention). Changing the date without changing the content doesn't count — AI crawlers are smarter than that.

**Tier assignment happens during the monthly Search Console review.** Pages that gain impressions get promoted. Pages that flatline get demoted. Data drives the schedule.

Update `dateModified` in frontmatter with every substantive edit.

## Part 11: Pre-Draft Research Protocol (REQUIRED)

> **Added March 29, 2026.** Every piece of content must go through this protocol before outlining begins. No exceptions. The research shapes the outline — not the other way around. This is the difference between writing what we think golfers want and writing what we can prove they're searching for.

### Why This Exists

We were writing content informed by intuition and our game-aliases doc. That produced good content, but it left gaps: we weren't systematically checking what currently ranks, we weren't harvesting the actual queries people type, and we weren't studying what the top results cover so we could beat them. The Wolf "wolf points" signal from Search Console proved the point — that was a keyword we didn't know existed until we saw the data. This protocol catches those signals before we write, not after.

### Step 1: Keyword Harvesting (30 minutes)

Before writing a single word, build the complete keyword map for this piece.

**Google Autocomplete (Alphabet Method):**
Type the target keyword into Google followed by each letter a-z. Record every suggestion. Then try the keyword with common golf prefixes: "how to," "what is," "best," "rules for," "how to play," "how to bet on."

Example for a Nassau piece: "nassau golf a..." "nassau golf b..." through z, plus "how to play nassau," "nassau betting rules," "nassau press rules," etc.

**People Also Ask (PAA):**
Search the target keyword. Expand every PAA question. Click into 2-3 of them — Google loads new PAA questions when you do. Record all of them. These are real questions real people ask, and many of them become FAQ entries or H2 headings.

**Related Searches:**
Scroll to the bottom of the SERP. Record all related searches. These are variant phrasings and adjacent topics Google associates with your keyword.

**Search Console Check (for refreshes):**
If the page already exists, pull the Queries report for that page. Sort by impressions. Every query with impressions that we didn't explicitly target is a signal. These queries are people finding us by accident — build them into the refresh intentionally.

**Game Aliases Doc:**
Check `strategy/content/game-aliases.md` for all known alternate names. Verify each alias still autocompletes. Add any new ones discovered during autocomplete harvesting.

**Output:** A keyword brief documenting:
- Primary keyword (highest volume, best intent match)
- Secondary keywords (5-10 variants and related terms)
- PAA questions (all of them — these feed the FAQ section and H2 headings)
- Related searches
- New aliases discovered
- Any Search Console queries (for existing pages)

### Step 2: Competitor Content Audit (30 minutes)

Search the primary keyword. Open the top 5 ranking results. For each one, document:

**Content inventory:**
- URL, title, word count
- What topics/sections they cover
- What questions they answer
- What examples or math they include (if any)
- What they miss or get wrong
- Their schema markup (check via View Source → search for "application/ld+json")
- Whether they have FAQ sections

**Gap identification:**
After reviewing all 5, answer these questions:
- What do ALL of them cover? (Table stakes — we must cover this too)
- What do NONE of them cover? (Our opportunity to be the only result with this information)
- What do they cover poorly? (Surface-level explanations we can go deeper on)
- Do any of them show settlement math? (Almost certainly not — this is our consistent edge)
- Do any of them include interactive elements, tables, or tools?

**The displacement question:**
For each ranking result, ask: "Why would Google replace this result with our page?" The answer needs to be specific. "Because ours is better" isn't enough. "Because ours includes actual settlement math with named players, covers 3 variations they skip, and answers 8 questions they don't address" — that's a displacement case.

**Output:** A competitive brief documenting what we must include, what we can uniquely add, and the specific reasons our content deserves to rank higher.

### Step 3: Search Intent Classification

Not every keyword has the same intent. Misreading intent means writing the wrong type of content.

**Informational:** "What is nassau in golf" → The searcher wants to learn. Write an explainer. Lead with the definition. This is most of our game guide traffic.

**Navigational:** "Stick golf app" → They're looking for us specifically. Homepage and app store links handle this.

**Commercial investigation:** "Best golf betting apps" → They're comparing options before buying. Write a comparison with honest pros/cons. This is our roundup post.

**Transactional:** "Download golf betting app" → They're ready to act. CTA-heavy landing pages, not long-form content.

**The SERP tells you the intent.** If the top results are all listicles, Google thinks the intent is comparison. If they're all how-to guides, the intent is educational. If they're product pages, the intent is transactional. Match what Google is already rewarding — don't fight the SERP.

**Output:** One sentence declaring the search intent and what content type matches it.

### Step 4: Internal Linking Pre-Plan

Before writing, map the link architecture for this piece. This happens before the outline so links can be woven naturally into the structure, not bolted on afterward.

**Outbound links (from this new piece):**
- Which existing Stick pages should this piece link to? Minimum 5.
- For each link, note which section of the new piece it belongs in and what the natural anchor text would be.
- Include at least 1 outbound link to an external authoritative source (USGA, PGA Tour, established golf publication).

**Inbound links (from existing pages TO this new piece):**
- Which existing pages should add a link to this new piece once published?
- For each, note where in the existing page the link fits naturally and draft the anchor text.
- This becomes the post-publish linking pass checklist.

**Hub connection:**
- Which content cluster does this piece belong to? (Game guides cluster, betting strategy cluster, app comparison cluster)
- Does it connect to the hub page for that cluster?
- Does it create a bridge between two clusters? (e.g., a "Hammer vs Press" post bridges Wolf and Nassau clusters)

**Output:** A link map with two columns — outbound links planned for the new piece, and inbound links to add to existing pages after publishing.

### Step 5: Schema Strategy

Decide which structured data schemas this specific piece needs. Not every page gets the same treatment.

**What's currently working for us (keep using):**

| Schema Type | When to Use | Status |
|---|---|---|
| **Article** | Every page (games, blog, guides) | Active on all pages |
| **FAQPage** | Every page with FAQ section | Active — no longer generates Google rich snippets for non-government sites, but gives 3.2x AI citation lift. This is our primary reason for using it. |
| **BreadcrumbList** | Every page | Active on all pages |
| **SoftwareApplication** | Homepage only | Active |

**What NOT to use:**

| Schema Type | Why Not |
|---|---|
| **HowTo** | Google deprecated HowTo rich results entirely (2023-2024). No visual benefit. Don't add it to game guides. |
| **VideoObject** | Only when we actually have embedded video content |

**Per-piece schema decisions:**
- Blog posts: Article + FAQPage + BreadcrumbList (standard)
- Game guides: Article + FAQPage + BreadcrumbList (standard)
- Reference guides: Article + FAQPage + BreadcrumbList (standard)
- Homepage: Organization + SoftwareApplication + BreadcrumbList

**Schema quality rules:**
- Article `dateModified` must reflect the actual last substantive edit, not the build date
- FAQPage answers must match the inline FAQ content exactly (frontmatter = source of truth)
- All schema must validate clean in Google's Rich Results Test (no errors, warnings acceptable)
- Author/publisher must consistently be `"Stick Golf"` with `url: "https://stickapp.golf"`

**Output:** A one-line note in the content brief confirming which schemas apply (usually "standard: Article + FAQ + Breadcrumb").

### Step 6: App & Existing Content Loading (REQUIRED)

Before writing anything, Claude must have a complete picture of (a) what Stick actually does today and (b) what we've already published on the topic. This is not optional — it prevents false claims, avoids contradicting our own content, and ensures the new piece builds on rather than duplicates what exists.

**Part A: Read our own published content first.**

Before touching any external research or app audits, read every existing page on stickapp.golf that relates to the topic. This includes:

1. **Game guides** (`site/src/content/games/[slug].mdx`) — Read any game guide that covers mechanics relevant to this piece. For a TGL Hammer article, that means reading the Wolf guide (hammer section) and the Scotch guide (roll/hammer section) in full.
2. **Reference guides** (`site/src/content/guides/[slug].mdx`) — Read any reference guide the new piece should link to or build on. For anything involving presses or stakes doubling, read the Presses guide.
3. **Blog posts** (`site/src/content/blog/[slug].mdx`) — Read any existing blog post that overlaps with this topic to avoid repeating the same examples, angles, or settlement walkthroughs.
4. **Content pipeline** (`strategy/content/content-pipeline.md`) — Check if there are other planned pieces on adjacent topics. The new piece should complement them, not cannibalize them.

**Why this matters:** If the Wolf guide already explains hammer in 200 words with a settlement example, the TGL Hammer blog post should reference and link to that section — not rewrite the same explanation. If the Presses guide already defines what a press is, the Hammer vs Press post should link to it for depth rather than re-explaining from scratch. Reading our own content first also reveals natural linking opportunities and prevents the new piece from contradicting terminology, examples, or advice we've already published.

**Cannibalization check (CRITICAL):**

After reading all related published content, explicitly answer this question: **"Could Google confuse this new page with an existing page?"** If two pages target the same primary keyword or answer the same core question, they compete with each other instead of with competitors. This is called keyword cannibalization and it dilutes ranking power across both pages.

**How to check:**
- Compare the new piece's primary keyword against the `keywords` frontmatter array of every related published page
- If there's a match or near-match, you have potential cannibalization
- Check the new piece's core question (what the first 60 words answer) against the opening paragraphs of related pages

**What to do when overlap exists (and it often will):**

1. **Different intent = no problem.** The Wolf game guide targets "wolf golf game" (informational — how to play). A TGL Hammer blog post targets "tgl hammer meaning" (cultural — what is this thing on TV). Different intent, different searcher, no conflict. Document why they don't cannibalize in the content brief.

2. **Same intent but different angle = manage carefully.** If two pages could both rank for "hammer golf betting," decide which page is the canonical authority. The other page should cover the topic more briefly and link to the authority page for depth. Example: the Wolf guide owns "how hammer works in Wolf." The TGL Hammer post briefly explains the mechanic (2-3 sentences max) and links to the Wolf guide's hammer section for the full rules.

3. **Same intent, same angle = don't write it.** If the new piece would essentially be a rewrite of something we already have, either expand the existing page instead or find a genuinely different angle.

**The rule of thumb:** Every page on stickapp.golf should have a unique primary purpose that no other page serves. If you can't articulate why this new page exists independently of pages we already have, it shouldn't be a separate page.

**Output from Part A:** A note in the content brief documenting:
- Which existing pages were reviewed
- Any overlap identified and how it will be managed
- The unique angle this new piece provides that no existing page covers
- Specific sections from existing pages to link to rather than rewrite

**Part B: Read app audit materials.**

**For game-specific content (game guides, game blog posts, comparison posts):**

Read the relevant materials in this order:
1. **The game audit file** (`content/game-guides/[game]-audit.md`) — what the engine supports, what's configurable, known bugs, feature gaps
2. **The raw audit** (`content/game-guides/[game]-audit-raw.md`) if it exists — full Claude Code output with test counts and edge case detail
3. **The game doc** from the app repo (`Stick/docs/games/[game].md`) if accessible — the original rules the engine was built from
4. **The capabilities spec** (`Stick/docs/STICK-CAPABILITIES-SPEC.md`) if accessible — cross-game feature matrix

**For general content (beginners guide, app comparison, glossary):**
Read the capabilities spec and any relevant game audits to ensure claims are accurate across all 12 games.

**Why this matters beyond accuracy:**

Research in Steps 1-2 will surface how golfers play these games in the real world. Comparing that against what the app actually does reveals three categories of insight:

1. **Contradictions** — Research says most groups play a mechanic one way, but Stick does it differently. These need a content decision (explain both? lead with Stick's approach?) AND a product flag (should the app change?).

2. **Missing configurations** — Golfers commonly play with a setting or variation that Stick doesn't support. Flag for product roadmap. In content, document the variation honestly without claiming Stick handles it.

3. **New game types or features** — Research occasionally surfaces game types or hybrid formats that aren't in Stick's lineup. Flag these as potential product opportunities.

**Output:** A "Product Intelligence" section in the content brief:

```
PRODUCT INTELLIGENCE:
- Contradictions: [anything research shows that conflicts with how Stick works]
- Missing configs: [common real-world variations Stick doesn't support]
- Product opportunities: [new games, features, or formats worth considering]
- Relevant bugs: [any known bugs from audit that affect this content]
```

This section gets reviewed by Justin during the Research Review (Step 7) and relevant items get added to the app's issue tracker.

### Step 7: Research Review (Checkpoint with Justin)

**This is a mandatory pause between research and writing.** After completing Steps 1-6, Claude presents a summary to Justin for review before any outlining begins. This is not a rubber stamp — it's a brainstorming moment.

**What Claude presents:**

1. **Research summary** (3-5 key findings) — What did the keyword harvest and competitor audit reveal? What's the biggest opportunity? What surprised you?

2. **Strategic questions** — Any decisions that need Justin's input before writing. Examples:
   - "Research shows most groups play [mechanic] this way, but Stick does it differently. Do we lead with the common approach and explain Stick's version as an alternative, or lead with Stick's approach?"
   - "There are two strong angles for this piece — [angle A] targets higher volume but lower intent, [angle B] targets lower volume but the reader is closer to downloading. Which feels right?"
   - "Competitor X covers [topic] in a way that contradicts what our audit says. Should we address this directly?"
   - "I found a forum thread where golfers are confused about [thing]. This could be its own piece — worth adding to the pipeline?"

3. **Proposed angle and structure** — A 2-3 sentence pitch for the piece: "Here's what I think this should be, here's the angle, here's why." Justin can redirect before 1,800 words go in the wrong direction.

4. **Product intelligence flags** — Any contradictions, missing configs, or opportunities from Step 6.

5. **Content brief** — The assembled brief from all research steps, ready for Justin to skim.

**Justin's role:** Skim the summary, answer questions, approve or redirect the angle. This should take 5-10 minutes. If Justin has no changes, a quick "looks good, go" is all that's needed.

**What this prevents:**
- Writing a full draft that misses the real opportunity because Claude didn't know about a group chat conversation Justin had
- Content that contradicts a product decision Justin already made
- Wasted effort on an angle that doesn't fit the brand
- Missing a chance to flag a product improvement while the context is fresh

**Output:** Justin's approval (possibly with redirections) documented at the top of the content brief.

### Step 8: Content Brief Assembly

Combine all outputs from Steps 1-7 into a single content brief. The brief should contain:

```
CONTENT BRIEF: [Title]
Date: [Date]
Type: [Game guide / Blog post / Reference guide]
Justin approved: [Yes / Yes with notes]

PRIMARY KEYWORD: [keyword]
SECONDARY KEYWORDS: [list]
SEARCH INTENT: [informational / commercial / etc.]

COMPETITOR GAPS:
- [What top results miss that we'll cover]
- [What they cover poorly that we'll do better]
- [Our unique angle]

PAA QUESTIONS → FAQ CANDIDATES:
- [Question 1]
- [Question 2]
- [etc.]

INTERNAL LINKS OUT: [list of pages to link to]
INTERNAL LINKS IN: [list of existing pages to update post-publish]
EXTERNAL LINKS: [authoritative source(s) to cite]

SCHEMA: [Article + FAQ + Breadcrumb]

PRODUCT INTELLIGENCE:
- Contradictions: [list or "none"]
- Missing configs: [list or "none"]
- Product opportunities: [list or "none"]

DISPLACEMENT CASE: [1-2 sentences on why this deserves to outrank current results]

RESEARCH REVIEW NOTES: [Justin's feedback, redirections, or "approved as-is"]
```

Store completed briefs in `content/briefs/` for reference. These briefs become the institutional memory of why each piece was written the way it was.

### When to Skip Steps

**Blog posts about games we've already audited:** Skip Step 6 app context loading if the audit is recent (within 60 days) and no engine changes have shipped. Still do the full keyword + competitor research. The keyword landscape for a blog post is different from the game guide even if the topic overlaps.

**Content refreshes:** Skip the full competitor audit but do a fresh autocomplete + PAA harvest. Skip the Research Review if the refresh is minor (adding 1-2 FAQs, updating a date). Do the Research Review if the refresh changes the page's angle or adds a major new section.

**Time-sensitive cultural pieces (T1C):** Compress the timeline but don't skip the Research Review. Justin needs to approve the angle even on fast-turnaround pieces — a wrong take on a trending topic does more harm than a late but correct one.

**Retroactive audits:** Use an abbreviated version — keyword harvest + internal linking audit + FAQ check. Skip competitor audit unless the page is underperforming (low impressions relative to keyword volume). Skip Research Review unless audit reveals something surprising.

## Part 12: Building a Content Skill

To enforce consistency across every piece of content, we should build a Claude skill that acts as a writing partner — understanding our voice, structure, SEO requirements, and quality standards.

### What the Skill Should Do

The content skill would be invoked whenever we're writing game guides or blog posts. It would:

- Enforce the game guide section template (Part 2) or appropriate blog structure (Part 3)
- Load the alias map for the target game (Part 9) and verify aliases appear in opening, quick reference, and FAQ
- Check drafts against the Kill List (Part 5) and flag banned words/phrases
- Verify the first 60 words contain a direct answer with the target keyword
- Ensure settlement math is zero-sum
- Generate JSON-LD structured data (Article, FAQPage, BreadcrumbList) for each piece
- Verify internal link targets match the relatedness map from the technical SEO spec
- Check sentence length variation and paragraph length variation
- Flag sections that don't start with a standalone answer
- Generate meta descriptions within the 150-160 character range

### Skill Structure

```
stick-content/
├── SKILL.md              — Core instructions, voice guide, templates
├── references/
│   ├── kill-list.md      — Words/phrases to avoid
│   ├── game-template.md  — Game guide section template
│   ├── blog-templates.md — Blog post templates by category
│   ├── seo-checklist.md  — On-page SEO requirements
│   └── link-map.md       — Internal linking relatedness map
└── scripts/
    ├── check-killlist.py  — Scan draft for banned words
    ├── check-math.py      — Verify settlement zero-sum
    └── generate-schema.py — Generate JSON-LD for a page
```

### When to Build It

After we've written 2-3 game guides by hand and refined the process. The first few guides will teach us what the skill needs to enforce. Building the skill too early means encoding assumptions we haven't tested yet. Building it after Nassau, Skins, and Wolf means encoding patterns we know work.

### What the Skill Doesn't Do

The skill handles structure and consistency. It doesn't replace editorial judgment. Questions like "is this strategy tip actually good advice?" and "does this example feel realistic?" still need human evaluation. Justin's review step (Step 5 in the workflow) is where quality gets enforced at the editorial level.
