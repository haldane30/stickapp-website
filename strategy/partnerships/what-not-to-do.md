# What NOT to Do — Golf Media PR Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them

**Last Updated:** March 16, 2026

Real mistakes founders make when pitching to golf media. Learn from them.

---

## PITCHING MISTAKES

### ❌ Mistake 1: Generic Pitch to Multiple Outlets (The Spray & Pray)

**The bad pitch:**
```
Subject: New Golf Betting App

Hi,

We have a new golf betting app called Stick. It has 12 games, real-time settlement,
and clean UI. Launching for Masters Week.

Check it out. Let me know if interested in coverage.

—Justin
```

**Why it fails:**
- Proves you haven't read/listened to their recent work
- Doesn't reference why your product fits *their* audience
- No personalization = delete button
- 87% of journalists trash generic pitches

**How to fix it:**
```
Subject: Guest pitch — Designer built golf app to solve settlement disputes

Hi [Host Name],

I've been listening to [SPECIFIC EPISODE from last month] and loved your take on
[SPECIFIC MOMENT]. That's exactly the problem we built Stick to solve.

[Rest of personalized pitch]
```

**The rule:** Personalize every pitch. Reference specific recent episodes/articles. Show you did homework.

---

### ❌ Mistake 2: Pitching Without Listening (or Reading)

**The bad approach:**
- Find podcast name on Google → email without listening to even one episode
- Read publication masthead → pitch without reading a recent article
- Assume all golf media wants app coverage (they don't)

**Why it fails:**
- Host/editor can tell immediately
- Your pitch angle won't fit their coverage style
- You'll miss obvious reasons it's a bad fit
- You'll waste your one credibility shot with that contact

**How to fix it:**
- Listen to 3-4 recent episodes of any podcast you pitch
- Read 2-3 recent articles from any publication you pitch
- Note: "They covered X in episode #456. They interview founders about real problems, not just products."
- Use that research in your pitch

**The rule:** 30 minutes of homework per outlet before you pitch. Non-negotiable.

---

### ❌ Mistake 3: Long, Feature-Rich Pitch (Information Overload)

**The bad pitch:**
```
Subject: Stick Golf Betting App — Comprehensive Overview

Hi [Host],

Stick is a golf betting app with 12 games. Here's what makes it special:

1. Nassau: Full press chain support with hammer mode variants
2. Skins: Carryover logic with configurable escalation
3. Wolf: Rotation detection with 2-on-2 partnership modes
4. Snake: Per-hole tracking with multi-player scalability
[... continues for 400 words]

We're launching for Masters Week. Our metrics show [long technical breakdown].

The user interface includes [design details].

Hoping to discuss partnership opportunities.

—Justin
```

**Why it fails:**
- Nobody reads 400-word pitches. They get one chance (first 50 words).
- Too much information confuses the angle
- Features are boring. Problems are interesting.
- Makes you sound like you're pitching investors, not a media story

**How to fix it:**
```
Subject: Guest pitch — Designer built golf app to stop parking lot disputes

Hi [Host],

I've been listening to your show and loved [specific ep]. You hit on the exact
problem we built Stick to solve: golfers spend 5% of a round playing, 95% settling bets.

Every foursome plays by different rules. Skins carry over in one group, not in
another. Proximity is disputed constantly. Stick's game engine bakes your house rules
in and settles automatically.

Launching for Masters Week on a bootstrap. Thought your audience would find the
product angle interesting.

Available to record [date].

—Justin
```

**The rule:** Lead with the problem, not the features. Hook in 50 words or less.

### ❌ Mistake 4: Claiming You're "Disruptive" or "Transformative"

**The bad pitch language:**
- "Disruptive innovation in the golf betting space"
- "Paradigm shift for how golfers approach betting"
- "Revolutionary settlement verification"
- "The only app that truly understands golf"
- "Transforming the golf betting market"

**Why it fails:**
- Corporate jargon = auto-delete in golf media
- Golf media readers are skeptical of buzzwords
- You're a 1-person bootstrap, not a billion-dollar company
- Authenticity beats hype in this space

**How to fix it:**
- Drop the jargon
- Use plain language: "Golfers spend time arguing about who won. We stop that."
- Let results speak: "Users report zero disputes in their first 10 games."
- Admit limitations: "We handle settlement verification better than existing apps because we designed it specifically for golfers' house rules."

**The rule:** Speak like a designer, not an MBA. Authenticity + specificity > hype.

---

### ❌ Mistake 5: Making False Product Claims (Without Your Feature Audit)

**The bad claim:**
```
"Stick is the only golf betting app that handles press chains correctly."
```

**Why it fails:**
- You haven't verified this against the app's actual code
- If it's wrong, you look dishonest
- Journalists will fact-check you
- Sets false expectations in users' minds

**How to fix it:**
- Run your feature audit FIRST (Claude Code output)
- Verify every claim against the audit
- Use conditional language: "Most apps break under edge cases. Ours handles [specific case] because..."
- Be honest about limitations: "We don't support manual presses yet, but we handle auto-presses perfectly."

**The rule:** Never pitch without reviewing your feature audit. If you haven't run an audit, get one.

---

### ❌ Mistake 6: Pitching the Same Outlet Multiple Times After Rejection

**The bad approach:**
```
First pitch: March 25, no response
Second pitch (April 1): "Following up on my earlier pitch"
Third pitch (April 8): "New angle: Masters Week tie-in"
Fourth pitch (April 15): "Last chance before we go to press"
```

**Why it fails:**
- You're burning a contact
- Journalists will recognize you as the persistent guy
- Next time you have a real story, they'll ignore it
- Golf media is small; reputation matters

**How to fix it:**
- One initial pitch
- One follow-up after 3-5 days if no response ("Just following up in case it got buried in your inbox")
- Then: Let it go
- Move to next contact

**The rule:** One pitch + one follow-up. Then move on.

## TIMING MISTAKES

### ❌ Mistake 7: Pitching During Masters Tournament (April 7-13)

**The bad timing:**
- Send major feature pitch April 9 (tournament in full swing)
- Expect editor response within 24 hours
- Send multiple follow-ups during tournament week

**Why it fails:**
- Golf media is 100% focused on Masters coverage
- Editors are overwhelmed
- Your email is competing against Rory's swing changes, course updates, player news
- Lead times are already slow (editors won't even read your pitch until April 14)

**How to fix it:**
- **April 2-5 (1 week before):** Podcast pitches, digital outlet pitches
- **April 6 (2 days before):** Short news briefs to GOLF.com
- **April 7-13 (Masters week):** Avoid new pitches. Focus on pre-scheduled appearances only.
- **April 14+ (after):** Magazine feature pitches. Editors have breathing room.

**The rule:** Pitch before the event, not during. Masters Week is for appearances you've already scheduled.

---

### ❌ Mistake 8: Expecting Immediate Response

**The bad expectation:**
- Send pitch Monday, expect response Tuesday
- Get impatient after 48 hours
- Assume no response = rejection

**Why it fails:**
- Magazine editors handle hundreds of pitches/week
- Journalists work on editorial calendars weeks ahead
- Podcast hosts batch-record episodes
- 2-3 week response time is normal

**How to fix it:**
- **Podcast:** 1-2 week response time (they often pre-record)
- **Digital publication:** 2-3 day response time
- **Magazine (monthly):** 2-4 week response time
- **Wait at least 1 week before following up**

**The rule:** Patience. Expect slow responses. Assume no response ≠ rejection (just inbox saturation).

## RELATIONSHIP MISTAKES

### ❌ Mistake 9: Bad-Mouthing Competitors

**The bad pitch:**
```
"Unlike Beezer Golf, which has outdated content and no innovation, Stick is..."

"18 Birdies is GPS-focused and treats betting as an afterthought. We actually..."

"Skins App has partnerships but zero product differentiation. Here's what makes us better..."
```

**Why it fails:**
- Golf media is small; someone knows the competitor founder
- Sounds defensive
- Journalists prefer facts over criticism
- Makes you look insecure

**How to fix it:**
```
"Most existing golf betting apps handle settlement manually (user taps who won).
We designed ours to settle automatically based on house rules."

"While some apps focus on GPS/rangefinding, we focused on settlement verification."

"We're the only app architected for 30+ player club events, which nobody else touches."
```

**The rule:** Lead with what you do well, not what others do poorly.

---

### ❌ Mistake 10: Asking for a Favor vs. Pitching a Story

**The bad ask:**
```
"Would you consider featuring Stick? We're a bootstrapped team and any help would
be much appreciated. Could you do a post about our launch?"
```

**Why it fails:**
- You're asking them to do you a favor
- Journalists don't care about your fundraising status
- There's no story angle, just a product promo
- Sounds needy

**How to fix it:**
```
"I built a golf betting app that solves a specific problem every golfer faces:
settlement disputes. The founder angle (25 years in design → solo bootstrap) might be
interesting to your readers. Happy to chat about it."
```

**The rule:** Pitch a story, not a favor. Make it about their audience, not your launch.

## CONTENT MISTAKES

### ❌ Mistake 11: Corporate Speak Instead of Plain Language

**The bad language:**
- "Leveraging advanced settlement verification algorithms"
- "Maximizing user engagement through real-time synchronization"
- "Deploying game-theoretic frameworks to optimize dispute resolution"
- "Unlocking the potential of mobile-first betting experiences"

**Why it fails:**
- Sounds like you copied it from a VC pitch deck
- Golf media audience is golfers, not investors
- Authenticity is your moat; jargon kills it
- Journalists will rewrite it anyway (poorly)

**How to fix it:**
```
Instead of "leveraging advanced settlement algorithms":
"The app does the math so golfers don't have to argue about it."

Instead of "maximizing user engagement":
"Players see the same game state in real-time, so there's no dispute about who won."

Instead of "game-theoretic frameworks":
"We handle edge cases correctly because we actually play golf and know what disputes happen."
```

**The rule:** Explain your product like you're talking to a friend over beers, not a VC panel.

---

### ❌ Mistake 12: Not Knowing Your Differentiation

**The weak pitch:**
```
"Stick is a golf betting app with 12 games. We have a clean interface and fair pricing."
```

**Why it fails:**
- Every app has a "clean interface"
- Fair pricing is table stakes
- No specific reason to cover you vs. competitors
- Journalist can't angle a story

**How to fix it:**
```
"Stick handles settlement verification automatically. Most apps ask you to tap who won
(user input = disputes). We designed the engine to settle based on your house rules.
Proximity is our biggest innovation—manual entry solves the 'who hit it closest' dispute."
```

**The rule:** Know your 2-3 specific differentiators. Lead with them.

## OUTREACH MISTAKES

### ❌ Mistake 13: Emailing Wrong Contact

**The bad approach:**
- Email the CEO/general contact instead of the golf reporter
- Email the front desk instead of the editor
- Send to company email instead of reporter's direct email
- Assume "[Firstname][Lastname]@domain.com" without verification

**Why it fails:**
- General inboxes are black holes
- Wrong person won't pass it along
- Looks like you didn't do homework

**How to fix it:**
- Use media databases: MuckRack, Rephonic, MillionPodcasts
- Find specific journalist/host names
- Search for their email on their publication/show website
- Try Twitter if email isn't available (DM is slower but works)
- Verify email format before sending

**The rule:** Find the specific golf reporter/podcast host. Email them directly. Use databases.

---

### ❌ Mistake 14: Not Following Up Properly

**The bad follow-up:**
```
First email: March 25, no response
Second email (April 1): "Hi, did you get my email?"
Third email (April 5): "This is urgent—we're launching soon."
Fourth email (April 10): "Last chance or we're going elsewhere."
```

**Why it fails:**
- Desperate tone
- Creates negative impression
- Makes them ignore you more

**How to fix it:**
```
First email: March 25, pitch
Second email (March 28): "Just following up in case it got buried in your inbox.
Happy to chat anytime. —Justin"
[No response after that? Move on.]
```

**The rule:** One follow-up, then let it go. Don't be pushy.

---

### ❌ Mistake 15: No Call-to-Action or Next Steps

**The bad pitch ending:**
```
"Let me know if you're interested."

"Hope this is something you'd consider covering."

"Looking forward to hearing from you."
```

**Why it fails:**
- Vague. No clear next step.
- Puts burden on them to figure out what to do
- Easier to ignore

**How to fix it:**
```
"Happy to record a guest appearance [THIS WEEK / NEXT WEEK / WHENEVER WORKS].
Just let me know your availability and any prep you need. —Justin"

"Available for a 30-minute call this Thursday or Friday to discuss story angle.
What works better for your schedule?"

"Launching April 7. Happy to do an interview closer to launch date if timing works."
```

**The rule:** Make it easy for them to say yes. Specific date options, clear ask, low friction.

## PREPARATION MISTAKES

### ❌ Mistake 16: Not Prepping Talking Points

**What happens if you book a podcast appearance unprepared:**
- You ramble about features instead of telling a story
- Awkward silences
- Host doesn't know where to take the conversation
- Bad audio quality or technical issues
- Episode comes out poorly and doesn't drive downloads

**How to fix it:**
**Prepare before EVERY recorded appearance:**

1. **3-4 key talking points** (30 seconds each):
   - "What problem did you solve?" (settlement disputes)
   - "Why did you build this?" (founder story)
   - "What makes you different?" (automated settlement vs. manual)
   - "What's next?" (Future roadmap)

2. **2-3 real settlement examples** (have them written down, practice telling them):
   - Example: "A guy's Skins carried over. Another guy's didn't. Standard game, different rules. Stick lets you pick once."

3. **Talking points on specific games** (Nassau, Skins, Wolf):
   - Have a 1-minute explanation of each

4. **Data or results** (if you have them):
   - "5,000 downloads first week"
   - "Average session duration X minutes"
   - "Users report zero disputes"

5. **Call-to-action** (clear and simple):
   - "Download Stick, play 3 free rounds, see what you think"
   - Not a hard sell, just an invitation

**The rule:** Prep like you're giving a speech. Because you are.

---

### ❌ Mistake 17: Not Testing Audio/Tech Before Recording

**The bad situation:**
- You're on a podcast recording
- Your mic sounds tinny
- Your WiFi drops halfway through
- You forgot to mute Slack notifications
- Background is messy/unprofessional
- Audio recording is distorted

**Why it fails:**
- Bad audio = episode is hard to listen to = host regrets having you on = low engagement
- Looks unprofessional
- Can't be edited out easily

**How to fix it:**
**30 minutes before recording:**

1. Find a quiet room with soft furnishings (reduces echo)
2. Use a decent USB mic if you have one (Audio-Technica, Blue Yeti, etc.)
3. Test audio: record yourself, listen back
4. Close all apps except the call (email, Slack, calendar)
5. Silence phone
6. Run a speed test on your WiFi
7. Have a backup (mobile hotspot, move closer to router)
8. Do a 2-minute sound check with the host before recording starts

**The rule:** Test everything 30 minutes ahead. If it sounds bad, reschedule.

## PLATFORM-SPECIFIC MISTAKES

### ❌ Mistake 18: Wrong Pitch for the Platform

**Example mistakes:**
- Pitching a detailed technical story to a comedy podcast (wrong audience)
- Pitching a long-form essay to a daily news site (wrong format)
- Pitching product features to a golf course design podcast (wrong angle)

**How to fix it:**
- Understand each outlet's coverage style
- Tailor your angle to their audience
- Podcast = story-driven, founder angle, entertainment value
- News site = breaking news, timely hook, short format
- Magazine = feature story, depth, narrative

**The rule:** Match your pitch angle to the outlet's coverage style.

## WHAT SUCCESS ACTUALLY LOOKS LIKE

✓ **Good signs:**
- Host/editor responds with scheduling question
- They ask for background info or bios
- They request a specific episode angle
- They ask for social media handles / links

✓ **Not good signs:**
- Long silence (assume they're not interested; move on)
- Rejection email (take it gracefully, don't argue)
- Slow response after 2 weeks (they're not prioritizing; OK to follow up once more)

---

## Final Reminders

1. **Journalists and podcasters get a lot of pitches.** You're competing for attention. Make yours stand out by being specific, authentic, and personalized.

2. **Golf media is small and interconnected.** Treat every contact like a relationship, not a transaction. Generosity and respect compound.

3. **You have a strong hand.** Founder story is real, product is authentic, problem is specific. These beat hype every time.

4. **Timing is real but not destiny.** Masters Week is busy, but you can still get coverage. Plan for 4-6 weeks of outreach, not 2 weeks.

5. **DIY PR works if you do the homework.** Personalization, authenticity, specificity. That's the moat.
